# Applying One-Point Perspective to Simple Shapes

This lesson focuses on applying the principles of one-point perspective to create a 2D representation of a 3D object that conveys depth. We'll work with simple geometric shapes to understand how this technique manipulates lines and vanishing points.

## Understanding the Core Idea

One-point perspective is a drawing method where all parallel lines that recede from the viewer converge at a single point on the horizon line, known as the **vanishing point (VP)**. This creates the illusion of depth on a flat surface.

### Key Components:

*   **Horizon Line:** An imaginary horizontal line that represents the eye level of the viewer.
*   **Vanishing Point (VP):** The point on the horizon line where parallel lines appear to converge.
*   **Orthogonal Lines:** Lines that are parallel in 3D space but converge to the vanishing point in the 2D drawing.
*   **Frontal Plane:** The face of the object that is parallel to the viewer's plane of vision.

## Step-by-Step Application: Drawing a Cube

Let's apply these concepts to draw a cube using one-point perspective.

### When to Use This Technique:

This technique is ideal for drawing objects that have one face directly facing the viewer, with other faces receding from that frontal plane. Think of a road stretching into the distance, a building viewed from its front, or a simple box.

### Steps:

1.  **Draw the Horizon Line and Vanishing Point:**
    *   Lightly draw a horizontal line across your drawing surface. This is your horizon line.
    *   Choose a single point on this horizon line. This is your vanishing point (VP).

    ![Diagram showing a horizon line and a vanishing point marked on it.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/One-point_perspective_diagram.svg/1280px-One-point_perspective_diagram.svg.png)
    *(Image source: Wikimedia Commons)*

2.  **Draw the Frontal Face:**
    *   Draw the face of the cube that is parallel to your viewing plane. For a cube, this will be a square.
    *   Position this square either above, below, or intersecting the horizon line.

3.  **Draw the Orthogonal Lines:**
    *   From each of the three visible corners of the frontal square that recede from the viewer, draw a light line connecting to the vanishing point (VP). These are your orthogonal lines.

4.  **Define the Depth (Back Face):**
    *   To determine how far back the cube extends, draw two vertical lines that are parallel to the sides of the frontal square. These lines should intersect two of the orthogonal lines you drew in the previous step.
    *   The point where these vertical lines meet the orthogonal lines defines the back edge of the cube.

5.  **Complete the Back Face:**
    *   Draw a horizontal line connecting the points where the two vertical lines intersect the orthogonal lines. This completes the back face of the cube.

6.  **Erase Construction Lines and Refine:**
    *   Erase any construction lines that are not part of the final cube (the parts of the orthogonal lines that extend beyond the cube's back face).
    *   Darken the visible edges of your cube.

### Worked Example:

Imagine we want to draw a cube that sits on a surface below our eye level (horizon line).

1.  Draw a horizon line. Mark a VP on it.
2.  Draw a square *below* the horizon line. This is the front face.
3.  From the top-left, top-right, and bottom-right corners of this square, draw lines pointing to the VP.
4.  To define the depth, draw a vertical line upwards from the bottom-left corner of the front face, and another vertical line upwards from a point *on the bottom orthogonal line* that defines the back edge. These vertical lines should not be too long, determining the cube's depth.
5.  Connect the top of these vertical lines with a horizontal line. This line should intersect the two orthogonal lines drawn from the top corners of the front face.
6.  Erase the excess orthogonal lines and darken the visible edges.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

*   **Lines Not Meeting the VP:** Ensure all receding parallel lines are drawn directly towards the single vanishing point. Any deviation will break the illusion of perspective.
*   **Inconsistent Depth:** Be mindful of how you define the depth of your object. Using parallel vertical and horizontal lines for the back face helps maintain consistency.
*   **Ignoring the Horizon Line:** The horizon line is crucial for establishing the viewer's eye level. Objects above it will appear from a bird's-eye view, while objects below it are seen from a worm's-eye view.
*   **Overlapping Construction Lines:** Keep construction lines light initially and erase them carefully to ensure the final drawing is clean and the form is clear.

By practicing with these basic steps, you can begin to apply one-point perspective to more complex shapes and scenes, creating drawings that have a convincing sense of three-dimensional space.
