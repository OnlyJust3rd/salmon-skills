# Requirement Discovery Techniques

When designing interactive systems, understanding what users truly need is paramount. This isn't about guessing; it's about actively uncovering those needs through structured techniques. This lesson focuses on implementing various methods to discover these user and system requirements.

## Why Diverse Techniques?

No single technique is a silver bullet. Different methods reveal different facets of user needs and system constraints. Employing a mix allows for a more comprehensive understanding, minimizing the risk of overlooking crucial aspects.

## Key Requirement Discovery Techniques

Here are several practical techniques you can apply:

### 1. User Interviews

**What it is:** Direct conversations with potential or existing users to understand their goals, pain points, and current workflows.

**When to use:** Early in the design process, to get qualitative insights, and to explore complex user motivations.

**How to implement:**

*   **Prepare:** Define clear objectives for the interview. Develop a semi-structured interview guide with open-ended questions.
*   **Conduct:**
    *   Start with introductions and set expectations.
    *   Ask probing questions: "Why?", "Tell me more about that," "How do you currently do X?"
    *   Listen actively and observe body language.
    *   Avoid leading questions.
    *   Take detailed notes or record (with permission).
*   **Analyze:** Transcribe recordings (if applicable). Identify recurring themes, pain points, and expressed needs.

**Example Questions:**

*   "Describe your typical day when performing task X."
*   "What are the biggest frustrations you encounter with the current system/process?"
*   "If you could wave a magic wand, what would make task X easier?"

### 2. Surveys and Questionnaires

**What it is:** Collecting data from a larger group of users through structured sets of questions.

**When to use:** To gather quantitative data, validate hypotheses formed from interviews, and understand broader trends.

**How to implement:**

*   **Design:** Keep questions clear, concise, and unbiased. Use a mix of question types (multiple choice, rating scales, open-ended).
*   **Distribute:** Use online tools (e.g., Google Forms, SurveyMonkey) or paper-based methods. Target the appropriate user groups.
*   **Analyze:** Use statistical methods to analyze quantitative data. Review open-ended responses for qualitative insights.

**Best Practices:**

*   Pilot test your survey to catch any ambiguities.
*   Keep it short to maximize completion rates.
*   Clearly state the purpose of the survey.

### 3. Observation (Contextual Inquiry)

**What it is:** Watching users perform their tasks in their natural environment.

**When to use:** To understand actual behaviors, uncover workarounds users employ, and identify implicit needs that users might not articulate.

**How to implement:**

*   **Setup:** Gain access to the user's environment. Ensure you have permission and inform users you will be observing.
*   **Observe:** Be unobtrusive. Focus on *what* users do and *how* they do it. Look for hesitations, repeated actions, or signs of frustration.
*   **Ask (Sparingly):** If necessary, ask clarifying questions *after* observing a behavior, not during, to avoid interrupting their flow. Frame questions around "understanding your process."

**What to look for:**

*   Workarounds and shortcuts.
*   Physical and environmental factors.
*   Tools and artifacts used.
*   Sources of interruption or distraction.

### 4. Prototyping and Usability Testing

**What it is:** Creating a preliminary version of the system (a prototype) and testing its usability with users.

**When to use:** To test design ideas early and often, gather feedback on specific features, and identify usability issues before significant development.

**How to implement:**

*   **Create Prototype:** This can range from paper sketches to interactive digital mockups. Focus on the core user flows you want to test.
*   **Define Tasks:** Create realistic tasks for users to complete using the prototype.
*   **Conduct Test:** Have users think aloud as they try to complete the tasks. Observe their actions, listen to their comments, and note any difficulties they encounter.
*   **Analyze:** Identify patterns of errors, points of confusion, and suggestions for improvement.

**Key Point:** The goal is to learn and iterate, not to "pass" a test.

### 5. Document Analysis

**What it is:** Reviewing existing documentation related to the system or domain.

**When to use:** To understand existing processes, business rules, technical constraints, and historical context.

**How to implement:**

*   **Identify Sources:** Look for user manuals, technical specifications, process flow diagrams, reports, support tickets, and competitive analysis documents.
*   **Review and Synthesize:** Read through the documents, extracting key information about functional requirements, non-functional requirements, constraints, and existing systems.

**Example Documents:**

*   **Current system manuals:** How things are *supposed* to work.
*   **Support tickets:** Real-world problems users face.
*   **Business process models:** Formal descriptions of workflows.

## Putting it Together

As you apply these techniques, remember to:

*   **Combine methods:** Use interviews to explore, surveys to quantify, observation to see, and prototypes to validate.
*   **Document everything:** Keep detailed records of your findings, and make sure they are accessible to your team.
*   **Focus on the user:** Always center your efforts on understanding and addressing the needs of the people who will actually use the system.
