# Understanding Rotation in Geometric Transformations

Geometric transformations are ways to move, resize, or reshape figures. We've been exploring the fundamental ones, and today we're focusing on **rotation**.

### What is Rotation?

Rotation is a geometric transformation that turns a figure around a fixed point called the **center of rotation**. Imagine spinning a pinwheel or turning a steering wheel; that's a rotation!

Key characteristics of rotation:

*   **Angle of Rotation:** This is how much the figure is turned. It's measured in degrees (or radians) and can be clockwise or counter-clockwise.
*   **Center of Rotation:** This is the point that stays fixed during the rotation. All other points of the figure move in a circular path around this center.
*   **Direction:** Rotations can be either clockwise (like the hands of a clock) or counter-clockwise (the opposite direction).

### Recognizing Rotation

How can you tell if a transformation is a rotation? Look for these signs:

*   **Shape and Size Remain the Same:** A pure rotation doesn't stretch or shrink the figure. The angles and side lengths of the original figure are preserved in the rotated figure.
*   **Points Move in Circles:** Each point in the original figure moves along a circular arc, with the center of the circle being the center of rotation.
*   **Fixed Center:** There's a specific point that doesn't move.

### Examples of Rotation

**2D Example:**

Imagine a simple square. If you rotate it 90 degrees counter-clockwise around its center, it will look the same, but its orientation will have changed. The top edge might now be on the left.

**3D Example:**

Think about the hands of a clock. The hour, minute, and second hands all rotate around the center of the clock face. This is a real-world example of rotation in 3D space, though we often consider it in a 2D plane. Another example is a Ferris wheel rotating around its central axle.

### Defining Rotation

A rotation can be formally defined by:

*   **The center of rotation.**
*   **The angle of rotation.**
*   **The direction of rotation (clockwise or counter-clockwise).**

For example, a rotation of 90 degrees counter-clockwise around the origin (0,0) will move a point (x, y) to a new position (-y, x).

$$
\text{Rotation}(x, y) \rightarrow (-y, x) \text{ for } 90^\circ \text{ counter-clockwise around origin}
$$

### Common Rotations

*   **90-degree rotation:** A quarter turn.
*   **180-degree rotation:** A half turn. The figure appears upside down relative to its original position.
*   **270-degree rotation:** Three-quarters of a turn.

### Distinguishing Rotation from Other Transformations

It's important to remember that rotation is distinct from other geometric transformations:

*   **Translation:** This is just sliding a figure without changing its orientation. In translation, all points move the same distance in the same direction.
*   **Scaling:** This changes the size of a figure, making it larger or smaller. In scaling, distances from a center point change proportionally.

When you encounter a geometric transformation problem, ask yourself:

1.  Did the figure slide (translate)?
2.  Did the figure turn around a point (rotate)?
3.  Did the figure get bigger or smaller (scale)?

By asking these questions, you can accurately identify and define the type of transformation.
