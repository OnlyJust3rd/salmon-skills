# Genre Categorization Criteria in Game Design

To understand how games are grouped into genres, we need to explore the criteria used for this classification. This micro-skill is fundamental to understanding the broader landscape of game design. By grasping these criteria, you'll be able to identify and discuss various game genres more effectively.

## Why Categorize Games?

Categorizing games isn't just about labeling. It helps us:

*   **Understand Player Expectations:** When a player sees a game is a "Role-Playing Game" (RPG), they immediately have expectations about gameplay, narrative depth, and progression systems.
*   **Guide Game Development:** Developers use genre conventions as a starting point, knowing what players of that genre generally enjoy and what mechanics are expected.
*   **Market and Sell Games:** Publishers and retailers use genres to target specific audiences and make games discoverable.
*   **Analyze the Market:** Understanding genre trends helps identify popular styles and potential gaps in the market.

## Core Criteria for Genre Classification

While some games blend genres, most can be categorized based on a few key principles. These principles focus on the *what* and *how* of the player's interaction with the game.

### 1. Core Gameplay Mechanics

This is arguably the most important criterion. It refers to the fundamental actions the player performs repeatedly throughout the game.

*   **What does the player *do* most often?** Are they shooting, building, solving puzzles, making strategic decisions, exploring, or interacting with characters?
*   **Examples:**
    *   **Shooter:** Primary mechanic is aiming and firing weapons. (e.g., *Doom*, *Call of Duty*)
    *   **Puzzle:** Primary mechanic is solving logic problems or manipulating objects to achieve a goal. (e.g., *Tetris*, *Portal*)
    *   **Strategy:** Primary mechanic is planning, resource management, and tactical/strategic decision-making. (e.g., *StarCraft*, *Civilization*)
    *   **Platformer:** Primary mechanic involves precise jumping and navigating environments. (e.g., *Super Mario Bros.*, *Celeste*)

### 2. Player Goals and Objectives

What is the overarching aim of the player within the game?

*   **What is the player trying to achieve?** Is it to defeat an enemy, complete a level, build an empire, tell a story, or survive?
*   **Examples:**
    *   **Real-Time Strategy (RTS):** Goal is to build a base, gather resources, and destroy opponent's forces, all in real-time.
    *   **Role-Playing Game (RPG):** Goal is typically to develop a character, complete quests, and often defeat a main antagonist, experiencing a narrative.
    *   **Survival:** Goal is to gather resources, craft tools, and stay alive against environmental or enemy threats.

### 3. Player Interaction and Control

How does the player influence the game world, and what is their perspective?

*   **Perspective:** Is the game viewed from first-person (eye-level), third-person (over the shoulder), top-down, or isometric?
*   **Pace:** Is the game real-time or turn-based?
*   **Examples:**
    *   **First-Person Shooter (FPS):** Player sees through the character's eyes and the core mechanic is shooting.
    *   **Turn-Based Strategy (TBS):** Players take turns making decisions, allowing for more deliberate planning.
    *   **Simulation:** Player typically manages or controls a system, often from a detached perspective.

### 4. Thematic Elements and Setting

While not as dominant as mechanics, the theme and setting can heavily influence a genre's identity and player perception.

*   **What is the game about thematically?** Is it fantasy, sci-fi, historical, horror, or realistic?
*   **Where does the game take place?** A medieval kingdom, a spaceship, a modern city?
*   **Examples:**
    *   **Fantasy RPG:** Often features magic, mythical creatures, and medieval-inspired settings.
    *   **Sci-Fi Shooter:** Features advanced technology, aliens, and futuristic or space settings.
    *   **Horror Survival:** Emphasizes fear, suspense, and often grotesque themes with limited resources.

## The Nuances of Genre Blending

It's important to recognize that very few games fit perfectly into a single box. Many games borrow elements from multiple genres, creating hybrid or sub-genres.

*   **Action RPG (ARPG):** Combines the combat and action of an action game with the character progression and questing of an RPG. (e.g., *Diablo*, *Elden Ring*)
*   **Survival Horror:** Blends survival mechanics (resource management, scarcity) with the tension and fear of horror. (e.g., *Resident Evil*, *Alien: Isolation*)
*   **Grand Strategy:** Combines deep strategic planning with a grand scope, often over long periods. (e.g., *Europa Universalis IV*)

When encountering these hybrids, it's useful to identify the *primary* mechanics and goals that define the player's core experience.

## Key Takeaway

Understanding genre categorization hinges on recognizing the **core gameplay mechanics**, **player goals**, **interaction style**, and to a lesser extent, the **thematic elements**. By analyzing these criteria, you can effectively classify games and appreciate the diverse ways players engage with them.
