# Understanding Solution Interview Objectives

When we're building solutions, especially in User Experience (UX), we're not just creating features for the sake of it. We're aiming to solve real problems for real people. To make sure our proposed solutions actually hit the mark, we need to talk to those people. This is where **solution interviews** come in.

Think of a solution interview as a focused conversation with potential users *after* you have an idea of what your solution might look like. It's not about understanding their problems (that's for problem interviews), but about seeing if your proposed fix makes sense to them.

## What Are We Trying to Achieve? (The Objectives)

The core purpose of a solution interview is to **validate** your proposed solution with your target users. Validation means confirming that your idea is desirable, feasible, and usable *from the user's perspective*.

Here are the key objectives we aim for in solution interviews:

*   **Gauge Desirability:** Does the user actually *want* this solution? Does it address their needs in a way that feels valuable to them?
*   **Understand Perceived Value:** How much value does the user think this solution will bring? Are they willing to use it? Are they willing to pay for it (if applicable)?
*   **Identify Usability Hurdles (Early):** Even before extensive testing, users can often point out things that don't make sense or are confusing about your proposed solution. This helps catch obvious issues early.
*   **Gather Feedback on Fit:** Does the solution align with the user's existing workflows or mental models? Does it feel like a natural fit, or does it require a significant behavioral change?
*   **Refine the Solution Concept:** Based on user feedback, what needs to be tweaked, added, or removed from the solution idea to make it more effective and appealing?
*   **Build Confidence in the Direction:** Successful solution interviews give you the confidence to move forward with developing the solution. Conversely, they can save you from investing heavily in a solution that won't resonate.

## Why Not Just Build It and See?

You might wonder why we go through the trouble of interviews instead of just building something and getting user feedback later.

> The cost of changing a solution after it's built is exponentially higher than changing an idea.

Solution interviews are a crucial step in the Lean UX or Design Thinking process. They are a form of **early validation** designed to de-risk your development efforts. Imagine spending months building a complex feature, only to discover users don't understand it or don't need it in the way you envisioned. Solution interviews help prevent these costly mistakes.

## A Practical Example

Let's say you're building an app to help people manage their household chores. After understanding the problems of chore delegation and tracking, you've come up with a proposed solution: a feature where users can create "chore packages" (e.g., a "Weekly Cleaning" package) and assign them to family members with due dates.

In a solution interview, you'd present this *concept* to potential users. You might use:

*   **Wireframes:** Simple visual representations of the screens.
*   **Mockups:** More detailed visual designs.
*   **Prototypes:** Interactive versions that simulate the user flow.
*   **Even just a clear verbal explanation with sketches.**

The objective isn't to test the technical implementation, but to understand their reaction. You'd ask questions like:

*   "If you saw this feature, what would you expect it to do?" (Checks understanding and desirability)
*   "How would this fit into how you currently manage chores?" (Checks for fit and potential workflow disruption)
*   "What do you think of creating 'chore packages'?" (Gauges reaction to the core concept)
*   "Is there anything here that seems confusing or unnecessary?" (Identifies early usability hurdles)
*   "If this existed, how likely would you be to use it for your family?" (Assesses perceived value and desirability)

The feedback from these interviews will guide your next steps. Maybe users love the idea of packages but find the assignment process confusing. This feedback is gold, allowing you to refine the assignment flow *before* writing a single line of code.

## Key Takeaway

Solution interviews are a low-cost, high-impact way to get early validation for your proposed solutions. By focusing on objectives like desirability, perceived value, and early usability hurdles, you ensure you're building the *right* solution, saving time and resources down the line.
