# Number Theory Definitions

This lesson focuses on recalling fundamental concepts within number theory. Understanding these definitions is crucial for developing strong algorithmic thinking skills.

## What are Number Theory Definitions?

Number theory is a branch of pure mathematics devoted primarily to the study of the integers. It deals with properties of numbers, such as divisibility, prime numbers, and congruences. For algorithmic thinking, these concepts help us understand patterns, build efficient algorithms for problems involving integers, and analyze their complexity.

## Key Definitions

Here are some essential number theory definitions to remember:

*   **Even Number:** An integer that is divisible by 2. It can be expressed in the form \(2k\), where \(k\) is an integer.
    *   Examples: -4, 0, 2, 10, 100

*   **Odd Number:** An integer that is not divisible by 2. It can be expressed in the form \(2k + 1\), where \(k\) is an integer.
    *   Examples: -3, 1, 5, 11, 101

*   **Divisor (or Factor):** An integer \(d\) is a divisor of an integer \(n\) if \(n\) can be divided by \(d\) without leaving a remainder. This means \(n = dq\) for some integer \(q\).
    *   Example: The divisors of 12 are 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, and 12.

*   **Prime Number:** A natural number greater than 1 that has no positive divisors other than 1 and itself.
    *   Examples: 2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13

*   **Composite Number:** A natural number greater than 1 that is not prime. It has at least one divisor other than 1 and itself.
    *   Examples: 4 (divisors: 1, 2, 4), 6 (divisors: 1, 2, 3, 6), 9 (divisors: 1, 3, 9)

*   **Greatest Common Divisor (GCD):** The largest positive integer that divides two or more integers without a remainder.
    *   Example: The GCD of 12 and 18 is 6. (Divisors of 12: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12; Divisors of 18: 1, 2, 3, 6, 9, 18. Common divisors: 1, 2, 3, 6. Greatest is 6.)

## Practical Scenario

Imagine you are designing an algorithm to check if a given number `n` is prime. You would need to recall the definition of a prime number: it must be greater than 1 and only divisible by 1 and itself. Your algorithm might involve iterating through potential divisors and checking for remainders. Understanding the difference between prime and composite numbers is fundamental to this task.

Similarly, if you are developing an algorithm to simplify fractions, knowing how to find the Greatest Common Divisor (GCD) of the numerator and denominator is essential.
