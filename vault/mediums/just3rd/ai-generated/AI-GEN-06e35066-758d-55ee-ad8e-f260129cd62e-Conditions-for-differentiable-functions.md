# Differentiability: The Foundation for Calculus Theorems

Before we can confidently apply powerful calculus theorems like Rolle's Theorem or the Mean Value Theorem, we need to ensure our function meets a fundamental requirement: **differentiability**. This lesson focuses on recalling the essential conditions for a function to be considered differentiable.

## What Does Differentiability Mean?

Think of a differentiable function as a smooth curve. It doesn't have any sharp corners, breaks, or vertical tangents. At every point on the graph of a differentiable function, you can draw a unique tangent line. Mathematically, this means the derivative of the function exists at that point.

## The Key Conditions for Differentiability

For a function \(f(x)\) to be differentiable on an interval, two primary conditions must be met at every point within that interval:

1.  **Continuity:** The function must be **continuous** at the point. This means:
    *   The function is defined at the point.
    *   The limit of the function exists at the point.
    *   The limit of the function at the point equals the function's value at that point.

    In simpler terms, there are no jumps, holes, or gaps in the graph.

2.  **No Sharp Turns or Vertical Tangents:** The graph of the function must be **smooth**. This means:
    *   The derivative from the left at the point must equal the derivative from the right at the point.
    *   There are no abrupt changes in direction (like in an absolute value function at its vertex).
    *   There are no vertical tangents, where the slope would be undefined (infinite).

## Practical Scenario

Imagine you are analyzing the speed of a car over time. The speed function, \(v(t)\), needs to be differentiable for us to apply theorems about average speed or instantaneous speed changes.

*   If the car suddenly teleports to a new location (a **discontinuity**), its speed isn't well-defined at that instant, and the function is not continuous, let alone differentiable.
*   If the driver suddenly slams on the brakes, causing an immediate, sharp change in deceleration (a **sharp turn** in the "acceleration" graph, which is the derivative of velocity), the derivative might not be defined at that exact moment.
