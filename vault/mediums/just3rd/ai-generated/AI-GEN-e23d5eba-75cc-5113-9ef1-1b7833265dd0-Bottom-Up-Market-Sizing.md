# Bottom-Up Market Sizing

Understanding how large a market is can be a crucial part of competitor analysis. One common technique to estimate this is the "bottom-up" approach. This method involves starting with specific, identifiable units and building up to a total market estimate.

## What is Bottom-Up Market Sizing?

Bottom-up market sizing focuses on aggregating data from the most granular level. Instead of looking at broad industry trends, you identify specific customer segments or individual entities and then estimate their potential spending or consumption. By summing these individual estimates, you can arrive at a total market size.

This technique is particularly useful when you have detailed information about:
*   **Specific customer groups:** Who are your potential customers?
*   **Their needs or behaviors:** How often do they purchase a similar product or service? How much do they typically spend?
*   **The number of these customers:** How many of these specific groups exist?

## How to Apply Bottom-Up Market Sizing

The core idea is to break down the market into manageable pieces and then reassemble them. Here's a simplified process:

1.  **Identify your target customer segment(s):** Define the specific groups of people or businesses who would use your product or service. Be as precise as possible.
2.  **Estimate the number of potential customers in each segment:** This might involve using publicly available data, conducting surveys, or making informed assumptions.
3.  **Estimate the average revenue or usage per customer:** Determine how much each customer in a segment would likely spend or use your product/service annually.
4.  **Calculate the total market size for each segment:** Multiply the number of customers by the average revenue/usage per customer.
5.  **Sum the market sizes of all segments:** Add up the totals from each segment to get your overall market size estimate.

## Practical Example: Estimating the Market for a New Local Coffee Shop

Let's say you're opening a new independent coffee shop in a medium-sized city. Here’s how you could use a bottom-up approach:

**1. Identify Target Customer Segments:**
*   **Segment A: Daily Commuters:** People who pass through the area on their way to work and might grab a coffee.
*   **Segment B: Local Office Workers:** Employees in nearby office buildings who might buy coffee during breaks or lunch.
*   **Segment C: Weekend Visitors/Residents:** People in the neighborhood on Saturdays and Sundays looking for a place to relax.

**2. Estimate the Number of Potential Customers:**
*   **Segment A (Commuters):** You observe traffic counts or use local transport data to estimate that 5,000 people commute past your potential location daily. Assuming 20% might stop for coffee: \(5,000 \times 0.20 = 1,000\) potential daily commuter customers.
*   **Segment B (Office Workers):** You find there are 3 office buildings within a 5-minute walk, housing approximately 800 employees. Assuming 40% might buy coffee from your shop: \(800 \times 0.40 = 320\) potential daily office worker customers.
*   **Segment C (Weekend Visitors/Residents):** This is harder to quantify precisely, but based on foot traffic observations on a typical weekend, you estimate an average of 500 people per day who might visit.

**3. Estimate Average Revenue/Usage Per Customer (Daily):**
*   **All Segments:** You assume the average customer spends $5.00 per visit on coffee and a pastry.

**4. Calculate Total Market Size for Each Segment (Daily):**
*   **Segment A:** \(1,000 \text{ customers} \times \$5.00/\text{customer} = \$5,000\)
*   **Segment B:** \(320 \text{ customers} \times \$5.00/\text{customer} = \$1,600\)
*   **Segment C:** \(500 \text{ customers} \times \$5.00/\text{customer} = \$2,500\)

**5. Sum the Market Sizes (Daily):**
*   Total Daily Market: \(\$5,000 + \$1,600 + \$2,500 = \$9,100\)

To get an annual market size, you'd then multiply this daily figure by the number of operating days in a year (e.g., 350 days if closed 15 days): \(\$9,100/\text{day} \times 350 \text{ days/year} = \$3,185,000\)

This bottom-up approach provides a grounded estimate by starting with real (or reasonably estimated) customer numbers and their spending habits.
