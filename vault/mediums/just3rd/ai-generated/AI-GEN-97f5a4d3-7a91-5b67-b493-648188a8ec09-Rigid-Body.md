# Rigid Bodies in Game Physics

In game development, when we want objects to behave realistically under forces like gravity, collisions, or explosions, we often use the concept of **rigid bodies**. A rigid body is a fundamental building block for simulating physical interactions in a game.

### What is a Rigid Body?

Imagine a real-world object, like a wooden block or a metal ball. If you push it, it moves. If it hits another object, both might bounce off. If you drop it, it falls. These are all characteristics of how physical objects interact.

In the context of game physics, a **rigid body** is a simplified representation of a physical object that has:

*   **Mass:** How much "stuff" the object is made of. This affects how easily it accelerates when a force is applied (Newton's second law: \(F=ma\)). A heavier object requires more force to move.
*   **Inertia:** The resistance of an object to changes in its state of motion. This is closely related to mass and also to how the mass is distributed (its shape and size).
*   **Position and Orientation:** Where the object is in the game world and how it is rotated.
*   **Velocity and Angular Velocity:** How fast and in what direction the object is moving (linear velocity) and how fast it is rotating (angular velocity).

The key characteristic of a rigid body is that its **shape does not deform**. Unlike a rubber ball that might squish on impact, a rigid body maintains its form. This simplification is crucial because simulating deformable objects is significantly more complex and computationally expensive.

### Why Use Rigid Bodies?

Rigid bodies allow game engines to:

*   **Simulate Realistic Motion:** Objects will fall due to gravity, roll down slopes, and be pushed by forces.
*   **Handle Collisions:** When two rigid bodies collide, their interaction can be calculated, leading to bouncing, sliding, or stopping.
*   **React to Forces:** Explosions, wind, or character actions can apply forces to rigid bodies, making them react dynamically.
*   **Create Interactive Environments:** Players can push boxes, knock over barrels, or send debris flying, making the game world feel more alive and responsive.

### Key Concepts Related to Rigid Bodies

#### Forces and Torques

Forces are what cause linear motion (pushing or pulling). In games, gravity is a constant downward force. When you hit a rigid body, you apply a force to it.

*   **Force (F):** A push or pull that can change an object's linear velocity.
*   **Torque ($\tau$)**: A rotational force. It's what causes an object to start spinning or change its spin rate. Imagine trying to unscrew a jar lid – you apply torque.

#### Inertia Tensor

While mass determines resistance to linear acceleration, the **inertia tensor** describes an object's resistance to *angular* acceleration. It takes into account not just the total mass, but also how that mass is distributed relative to an axis of rotation. A long, thin rod will rotate differently than a compact sphere of the same mass when the same torque is applied.

#### Collision Detection and Response

Rigid bodies are central to collision systems:

1.  **Collision Detection:** The game engine needs to figure out *if* and *when* two rigid bodies are overlapping or about to touch.
2.  **Collision Response:** Once a collision is detected, the engine calculates how the objects should react. This typically involves:
    *   **Impulses:** Instantaneous changes in momentum (and thus velocity) that occur during a collision. These are what cause bouncing.
    *   **Friction:** A force that opposes motion between surfaces in contact.
    *   **Restitution (Bounciness):** A value that determines how much kinetic energy is preserved during a collision. A restitution of 1 means a perfectly elastic collision (like a super bouncy ball), while 0 means a perfectly inelastic collision (they stick together).

### Example: A Simple Ball Dropped

Consider a simple spherical rigid body representing a ball.

1.  **Initialization:** The ball is given a mass (e.g., 1 kg), an initial position (e.g., at a height of 10 units above the ground), and an initial velocity of zero.
2.  **Gravity:** The physics engine continuously applies a downward force (gravity) to the ball.
3.  **Motion Update:** In each frame of the game, the engine uses the applied forces (gravity) and the ball's mass to calculate its acceleration. This acceleration is then used to update the ball's linear velocity, and the velocity is used to update its position.
4.  **Ground Collision:** When the ball's position indicates it has hit the ground (another object, possibly also a rigid body), collision detection identifies the contact.
5.  **Collision Response:** A collision response calculates an impulse that pushes the ball back upwards, reducing its downward velocity. The restitution value determines how high it bounces. If the restitution is low, it might just settle on the ground.

### Common Pitfalls

*   **Overly Complex Geometry:** Using highly detailed meshes for rigid body calculations can be computationally expensive. Often, simpler "collider shapes" (like spheres, boxes, or capsules) are used for collision detection, even if the visual mesh is more complex.
*   **Ignoring Torques:** For objects that can spin or be acted upon by rotational forces, neglecting torque calculations will lead to unrealistic behavior.
*   **"Tunneling":** If objects are moving very fast and the simulation step (frame rate) is low, a fast-moving rigid body might pass *through* another object without a collision being detected. This requires careful tuning of physics settings and potentially continuous collision detection.

Understanding rigid bodies is the first step towards building dynamic and engaging physical interactions within your game.
