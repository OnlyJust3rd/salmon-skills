# Initial Interface Concepts

Welcome to this lesson on creating initial interface concepts. As part of our work in Human-Computer Interaction, we'll be focusing on how to take an idea and translate it into rough, preliminary designs for a computer-based device. This is a crucial step in the design process, allowing us to explore possibilities before committing to detailed mockups.

## What are Initial Interface Concepts?

Initial interface concepts are the very first, often low-fidelity, representations of how a user will interact with a digital product. Think of them as sketches, wireframes, or even just rough diagrams that capture the core ideas of your interface. They are not meant to be polished or perfect; their purpose is to:

*   **Explore ideas:** Generate a variety of potential solutions.
*   **Communicate early:** Share your vision with your team quickly.
*   **Identify core functionality:** Focus on what the interface needs to *do*.
*   **Gather feedback:** Get initial reactions and iterate on your ideas.

These concepts are the foundation upon which more detailed designs will be built. They help us apply core UX principles by forcing us to think about how users will navigate, understand, and accomplish tasks within the digital environment.

## When to Use Initial Interface Concepts

You should generate initial interface concepts at the very beginning of a design project, once you have a basic understanding of the user needs and the overall goals of the product. This phase is ideal for:

*   **Brainstorming sessions:** To quickly capture and visualize a flood of ideas.
*   **Early project planning:** To align the team on a general direction.
*   **Exploring different approaches:** When you have multiple ways to solve a user problem.

## Creating Your Initial Interface Concepts: A Step-by-Step Approach

Let's walk through a practical way to generate these concepts. We'll assume you're working on a team project and have a general idea of the problem you're trying to solve.

### Step 1: Define the Core Task(s)

Before you draw anything, clearly identify the primary actions a user needs to perform with your interface.

**Example:** If you're designing an app for ordering coffee, the core tasks might be:
*   Browse menu items.
*   Customize an order (size, milk, additions).
*   Add items to a cart.
*   Complete checkout.

### Step 2: Sketching - The "Crazy 8s" Method

A popular and effective technique for rapid concept generation is "Crazy 8s." This method encourages you to think broadly and quickly.

**How it works:**

1.  **Get a piece of paper:** Fold it into eight equal sections. A simple letter-sized paper folded twice will do.
2.  **Set a timer:** Give yourself just 1 minute per section (8 minutes total).
3.  **Sketch one idea per section:** In each section, quickly draw a distinct idea for how to present a particular screen or interaction. Don't worry about perfection; focus on conveying the basic layout and elements.
4.  **Vary your ideas:** Try different approaches in each section. Think about where buttons should go, how information is displayed, and the general flow.

**Example Application for Coffee App (focusing on the "Browse Menu" task):**

*   **Section 1:** A simple list of coffee types with images.
*   **Section 2:** A grid of coffee cards with price and a "quick add" button.
*   **Section 3:** A categorized menu with tabs at the top.
*   **Section 4:** A visual carousel of popular drinks.
*   **Section 5:** A search bar prominently displayed with category filters below.
*   **Section 6:** A layout prioritizing images and larger "tap to select" areas.
*   **Section 7:** A more text-heavy list for users who prefer details.
*   **Section 8:** A completely different approach, perhaps voice-command driven browsing.

### Step 3: Visualizing Key Screens and Flows

After your rapid sketching, select the most promising ideas or elements from your Crazy 8s. Now, you'll flesh these out a bit more to represent a key screen or a simple user flow.

**Tools:**

*   **Pen and Paper:** Still the best for initial concepts.
*   **Whiteboard:** Great for collaborative sketching.
*   **Low-fidelity digital tools:** Tools like Balsamiq, Figma (in wireframe mode), or even simple drawing programs can be used, but keep it quick and unpolished.

**What to include:**

*   **Basic layout:** Where will the main content, navigation, and action buttons be?
*   **Key elements:** What are the essential UI components (buttons, text fields, images, lists)?
*   **Labels:** Briefly label important elements so their purpose is clear.
*   **User flow (optional but helpful):** Draw arrows to show how a user might move from one screen or state to another.

**Example: A " Customize Order" Screen Concept (building on the coffee app):**

Imagine you chose a card-based layout from your Crazy 8s. Your sketch might show:

*   A large image of the selected coffee at the top.
*   Below the image, clear options for "Size" (Small, Medium, Large buttons).
*   Next, "Milk" (options like Whole, Skim, Oat, Almond).
*   Then, "Add-ins" (a list of checkboxes or selectable items like Extra Shot, Syrup, Whipped Cream).
*   Finally, a prominent "Add to Cart" button at the bottom.

You might draw a faint arrow from this screen leading to a "Cart" icon in a hypothetical header, indicating the next step.

### Step 4: Present and Discuss with Your Team

Share your initial concepts with your team. The goal isn't to defend your ideas, but to get collective input and identify the strongest directions.

*   **Explain your thinking:** Briefly describe the core task you were addressing and why you chose this approach.
*   **Ask for specific feedback:** "Does this layout make sense for browsing?" or "Is it clear how to customize the drink?"
*   **Identify common themes:** See if multiple team members have similar ideas or concerns.

## Common Pitfalls to Avoid

*   **Over-detailing:** Don't spend too much time on colors, fonts, or perfect spacing. This is not the stage for that.
*   **Designing in a vacuum:** Always involve your team and consider user feedback early.
*   **Fixating on one idea too soon:** Explore multiple possibilities before settling.
*   **Forgetting the core task:** Ensure your concepts directly address the user's needs and goals.

By focusing on generating and refining these initial interface concepts, you lay a strong groundwork for a user-centered design. This process helps you explore the "what" and "how" of your interface in a quick, iterative, and collaborative manner.
