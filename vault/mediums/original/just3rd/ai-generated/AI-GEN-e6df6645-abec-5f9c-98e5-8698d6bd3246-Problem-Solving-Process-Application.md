# Applying the Problem-Solving Process: Defining and Specifying

This lesson focuses on applying the systematic steps of the engineering problem-solving process. We'll walk through how to effectively define an engineering challenge and specify its essential requirements. This is a crucial step in the engineering process, ensuring we tackle the right problem with clear goals.

## The Foundation: Understanding the Problem

Before jumping into solutions, we must clearly understand what we're trying to solve. This involves:

1.  **Identifying the Core Issue:** What is the fundamental problem or need?
2.  **Gathering Information:** What do we know about the problem? What information is missing?
3.  **Defining the Scope:** What are the boundaries of this problem? What is *not* part of this problem?

## Step 1: Defining the Problem Statement

A well-defined problem statement is clear, concise, and specific. It acts as a guiding star for the entire engineering process.

**Key Elements of a Good Problem Statement:**

*   **Who:** Who is affected by the problem? Who is the user?
*   **What:** What is the specific issue or unmet need?
*   **Where:** Where does this problem occur?
*   **When:** When does this problem manifest? Are there any time constraints?
*   **Why:** Why is this a problem? What are the negative consequences of not solving it?

**Example Scenario:**

Imagine a local community garden struggling with consistent watering for its diverse plant species. Some plants need frequent watering, while others thrive with less. This leads to either over-watered plants wilting or under-watered plants dying, impacting the garden's overall yield and health.

**Applying the Problem Statement Elements:**

*   **Who:** The community gardeners and the plants themselves.
*   **What:** Inconsistent and often improper watering of plants in the community garden.
*   **Where:** The community garden.
*   **When:** During periods of varied weather (hot, dry spells, occasional heavy rain) and across different plant growth cycles.
*   **Why:** Leads to plant stress, reduced yield, increased plant loss, wasted water (in over-watering), and gardener frustration.

**Crafting the Problem Statement:**

> "Community gardeners at the Green Earth Community Garden face challenges in providing consistent and appropriate watering to a diverse range of plants, leading to plant stress, reduced yield, and increased plant loss, particularly during periods of fluctuating weather."

## Step 2: Specifying Requirements

Once the problem is defined, we need to outline what a successful solution *must* achieve. These are the requirements. Requirements can be functional (what the solution *does*) or non-functional (how well the solution performs).

**Types of Requirements:**

*   **Functional Requirements:** Describe the specific actions or functions the system/solution must perform.
    *   *Example:* "The watering system must deliver water to individual plant beds."
*   **Non-Functional Requirements:** Describe the qualities or constraints of the system/solution. These include performance, reliability, usability, maintainability, and cost.
    *   *Example:* "The watering system should be easy for gardeners to operate with minimal training."
    *   *Example:* "The watering system should operate within a budget of $500."

**Brainstorming Requirements for the Community Garden Watering Problem:**

Let's consider different categories of requirements for our watering system.

| Requirement Category | Specific Requirements                                                                                                                                                                                                                         | Type (Functional/Non-Functional) |
| :------------------- | :---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | :------------------------------- |
| **Water Delivery**   | - The system must be able to deliver water to at least 10 distinct plant beds. <br> - The system must allow for adjustable watering duration for each bed. <br> - The system must be able to deliver water at a flow rate suitable for drip irrigation. | Functional                       |
| **Control & Automation** | - The system should allow for scheduled watering. <br> - The system should have a manual override option for immediate watering.                                                                                                               | Functional                       |
| **Usability**        | - The control interface must be intuitive and easy to understand. <br> - Installation and setup should be straightforward.                                                                                                                         | Non-Functional                   |
| **Reliability**      | - The system should be resistant to common weather conditions (sun, rain). <br> - Components should have a lifespan of at least 5 years.                                                                                                               | Non-Functional                   |
| **Cost**             | - The total cost of materials and assembly should not exceed $500.                                                                                                                                                                               | Non-Functional                   |
| **Sustainability**   | - The system should be designed to minimize water waste.                                                                                                                                                                                        | Non-Functional                   |

**Key Considerations for Specification:**

*   **Measurable:** Can you verify if the requirement has been met? (e.g., "adjustable watering duration" is better than "flexible watering").
*   **Unambiguous:** Is there only one interpretation of the requirement?
*   **Achievable:** Is it realistically possible to meet this requirement with available resources and technology?
*   **Necessary:** Is this requirement essential to solving the problem?

## Practice Activity

Imagine you are designing a new mobile app for local event discovery.

1.  **Define the Problem Statement:** Who faces the problem, what is it, where, when, and why is it a problem?
2.  **Specify Key Requirements:** Brainstorm at least three functional and three non-functional requirements for this event discovery app. Ensure they are measurable and unambiguous.

By diligently defining the problem and specifying clear requirements, you lay a robust foundation for developing effective engineering solutions. This systematic approach helps prevent wasted effort and ensures your final product truly addresses the intended need.
