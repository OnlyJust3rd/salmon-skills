# The Purpose of Customer Discovery Interviews

In the Lean Startup methodology, a core idea is to learn quickly and efficiently. This means we don't want to spend a lot of time and money building something nobody wants. This is where **customer discovery interviews** come in.

## Why Talk to Customers?

Imagine you have a brilliant idea for a new product or service. You think it will solve a major problem for a specific group of people. Before you write a single line of code or design a single prototype, you need to know if your idea is actually valuable to those people.

Customer discovery interviews are your first, and arguably most important, tool for finding this out. Their primary purpose is to **validate your business hypotheses**.

### What's a Hypothesis?

A hypothesis is essentially an educated guess or an assumption you make about your business. In the context of customer discovery, these hypotheses usually revolve around:

*   **Problems:** Do people actually experience the problem you think they do? How severe is it?
*   **Solutions:** Does your proposed solution address their problem effectively?
*   **Customer Segments:** Is the group of people you're targeting the right one? Do they have the problem?
*   **Value Proposition:** Is your solution valuable enough for them to consider using or paying for it?

**Think of it this way:**

Your initial business idea is a set of assumptions. If these assumptions are wrong, your entire venture is built on shaky ground. Customer discovery interviews are designed to test these assumptions *early and often*.

### The "Build-Measure-Learn" Loop

Customer discovery interviews are a critical part of the **"Learn"** phase in the "Build-Measure-Learn" feedback loop.

1.  **Build:** You build a minimal version of your idea (or even just a concept).
2.  **Measure:** You gather data and insights from potential customers.
3.  **Learn:** You use those insights to understand what's working, what's not, and what to do next.

The interviews are how you gather those crucial insights. They help you avoid building something based on a flawed understanding of your customers and their needs.

## Key Goals of Customer Discovery Interviews

When you conduct customer discovery interviews, you're not trying to sell your product. You're trying to *learn*. Here are the main goals:

*   **Uncover Real Problems:** Are the problems you *think* exist actually felt by your target audience? How do they describe these problems in their own words?
*   **Understand Customer Language:** How do your potential customers talk about their challenges? This is invaluable for marketing and product messaging later on.
*   **Validate or Invalidate Hypotheses:** For each of your core assumptions (e.g., "Entrepreneurs struggle with lead generation"), you're seeking evidence to prove or disprove it.
*   **Identify Pain Points:** What are the biggest frustrations, inefficiencies, or unmet needs your target customers experience?
*   **Discover Existing Solutions:** How are people currently trying to solve these problems? What do they like and dislike about those solutions?
*   **Assess the Importance of the Problem:** Is this a "must-have" problem or a "nice-to-have" inconvenience? This helps determine the market potential.
*   **Refine Your Understanding of the Customer:** You might discover your target customer is different from who you initially thought.

By asking the right questions and actively listening, you gain a deep understanding of your customer's world. This knowledge allows you to pivot or persevere your business idea, significantly increasing your chances of success.

### A Common Pitfall: Selling Too Early

A frequent mistake is treating customer discovery interviews like sales calls. You might be tempted to talk about your amazing solution and its features. Resist this urge! The purpose at this stage is pure learning. If you start selling, customers will likely tell you what they *think* you want to hear to be polite, rather than giving you honest feedback about their problems.

By focusing on understanding your customer's problems and context *first*, you lay a solid foundation for building a product that truly resonates and solves a real need.
