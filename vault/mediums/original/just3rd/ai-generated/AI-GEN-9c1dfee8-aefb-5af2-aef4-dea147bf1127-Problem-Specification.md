# Defining Problems: Inputs, Outputs, and Constraints

Computational problem solving starts with clearly understanding the problem you're trying to solve. A crucial first step is **Problem Specification**, where we define what information goes into the problem, what we expect to get out, and what rules or limitations apply.

## What is Problem Specification?

Problem Specification is about precisely describing a problem so that a computer (or even another person) can understand what needs to be done. It involves identifying three key elements:

*   **Inputs:** These are the data or information that you provide to the problem. Think of them as the ingredients you start with.
*   **Outputs:** This is the result or answer that you expect to get after processing the inputs. This is the finished dish.
*   **Constraints:** These are the rules, limitations, or conditions that must be met. They define the boundaries within which the solution must operate.

## Practical Example: Calculating the Area of a Rectangle

Let's say our computational problem is to calculate the area of a rectangle.

**Problem:** Calculate the area of a rectangle.

**Inputs:**
To calculate the area of a rectangle, we need to know its dimensions.
*   Length of the rectangle
*   Width of the rectangle

**Outputs:**
The desired result is the calculated area.
*   The area of the rectangle

**Constraints:**
For a rectangle, the length and width must be positive numbers. We can't have a rectangle with a negative length.
*   Length must be greater than 0.
*   Width must be greater than 0.

In a computational context, we might express this problem as:

Given two positive numbers, `length` and `width`, compute `area`.

The relationship between inputs and outputs is usually defined by a formula or a set of logical steps. For our rectangle example, the formula is:

`area = length * width`

**Constraints:**
*   Length must be a positive number.
*   Width must be a positive number.

The formula to calculate the area is `area = length * width`.

## Practice Task

Define the inputs, outputs, and constraints for a problem where you need to calculate the average of two numbers.

## Self-Check Questions

1.  What are the three main components of a problem specification?
2.  If you are asked to calculate the total cost of items, what would be the likely inputs and outputs?
3.  Why are constraints important in computational problem solving?
4.  Think of a simple task you do every day (e.g., making a sandwich). Can you break it down into inputs, outputs, and constraints?
