# Applying Problem Definition in Engineering Scenarios

Welcome to this lesson focused on applying problem definition within the engineering process. We'll move beyond understanding the concept to actively practicing how to define an engineering problem and its core requirements for a given situation.

## The Goal: Defining the "What" and "Why"

When we talk about problem definition in engineering, we're aiming to clearly articulate:

1.  **What is the problem?** This involves understanding the undesirable situation or the unmet need.
2.  **Why is it a problem?** This means understanding the impact, the consequences, and the stakeholders affected by the problem.
3.  **What are the core requirements for a solution?** This translates the problem into a set of measurable criteria that a successful solution must meet.

Applying this skill is crucial before jumping into designing or building anything. It ensures you're solving the *right* problem and have a clear target for your efforts.

## Scenario: Improving Urban Commuting

Let's imagine a scenario to practice our problem definition skills.

**Scenario:** You are part of an engineering team tasked with improving the daily commute for residents in a moderately sized city experiencing increasing traffic congestion. Commuters report long travel times, unpredictable arrival times, and a general feeling of stress related to their journey to work and back.

## Step-by-Step Problem Definition Application

Here's a structured approach to tackling this scenario:

### Step 1: Identify and Articulate the Core Problem Statement

Start by stating the problem as concisely as possible.

**Initial Thought:** Traffic is bad.

**Refinement (More specific):** Urban commuters in this city face significant delays and unpredictability in their daily travel due to increasing traffic congestion.

**Further Refinement (Focus on impact):** Increasing traffic congestion in the city leads to prolonged commute times, unpredictable arrival schedules, and heightened stress for urban commuters, negatively impacting their work-life balance and overall quality of life.

### Step 2: Identify Stakeholders and Their Perspectives

Who is affected by this problem? What are their specific concerns?

*   **Commuters:** Experience lost time, increased fuel costs, stress, and potential lateness for work.
*   **City Transit Authority:** May be struggling with existing infrastructure capacity, or looking for ways to encourage public transport use.
*   **Local Businesses:** Suffer from employee lateness, reduced productivity, and difficulties in attracting talent.
*   **Environmental Groups:** Concerned about increased emissions and pollution from idling vehicles.
*   **City Planners/Government:** Concerned about economic impact, resident satisfaction, and infrastructure sustainability.

### Step 3: Define the Scope of the Problem

What aspects of the commute are we focusing on? Are we looking at all modes of transport, or just private vehicles?

*   **Focus:** Daily travel to and from work for residents within the city limits.
*   **Exclusions (for now):** Long-distance travel, non-work related commutes, impact on freight transport (unless directly related to commuter flow).

### Step 4: Brainstorm Potential Causes (Briefly, to inform requirements)

While detailed root cause analysis is a separate step, a preliminary understanding helps frame requirements.

*   Increased number of vehicles on the road.
*   Insufficient road capacity or outdated infrastructure.
*   Lack of viable public transportation alternatives.
*   Poor traffic signal coordination.
*   Incidents (accidents, construction) causing disproportionate delays.
*   Urban sprawl leading to longer travel distances.

### Step 5: Define Measurable Requirements for a Solution

This is where we translate the problem into actionable criteria. What must a successful solution achieve?

| Requirement Category       | Specific Requirement                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                            | Measurement/Verification Method                                                                                                                                                                                                 |
| :------------------------- | :---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Travel Time Reduction**  | The proposed solution must reduce the average peak-hour commute time for affected residents by at least 20%.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                      | Before-and-after comparison of average commute times using GPS data from a representative sample of commuters, or through traffic flow simulations.                                                                                   |
| **Predictability**         | The variability in commute times (e.g., standard deviation of arrival times) must be reduced by at least 30% during peak hours.                                                                                                                                                                                                                   | Analysis of historical and post-implementation commute time data to calculate and compare standard deviations.                                                                                                                   |
| **Accessibility**          | The solution must be accessible to at least 80% of the city's residential areas within a 15-minute walk or bike ride to a point of access (e.g., transit stop, shared mobility hub).                                                                                                                                                               | Mapping of residential areas against the geographical coverage of the proposed solution's access points.                                                                                                                          |
| **Cost to User**           | The average daily cost for a commuter using the solution should not exceed the current average cost (fuel + vehicle wear and tear) by more than 10%, or should be demonstrably lower than current costs if the solution is public transit focused.                                                                                            | Cost analysis comparing current commuting expenses with projected costs of the new solution, considering fares, fuel, maintenance, or subscription fees.                                                                           |
| **Environmental Impact**   | The solution, when implemented at scale, should contribute to a reduction in greenhouse gas emissions from daily commutes by at least 15% compared to the current situation.                                                                                                                                                                    | Estimation of emissions based on mode shift projections and lifecycle assessment of the new infrastructure or service.                                                                                                             |
| **User Satisfaction**      | Post-implementation surveys should indicate an improvement in overall commuter satisfaction with their journey by at least one full point on a 5-point scale (e.g., from 2.5 to 3.5).                                                                                                                                                           | Standardized commuter satisfaction surveys conducted before and after implementation.                                                                                                                                             |

### Step 6: Document the Problem Definition

Consolidate your findings into a clear document. This might include:

*   **Problem Statement:** (As refined in Step 1)
*   **Stakeholder Analysis:** (Brief overview from Step 2)
*   **Scope:** (As defined in Step 3)
*   **Key Requirements:** (The table from Step 5)
*   **Assumptions:** (e.g., "We assume the city government will approve necessary infrastructure changes.")
*   **Constraints:** (e.g., "The budget for initial implementation is \$10 million.")

## Practice Activity

Now, take a moment to apply these steps to a different scenario.

**New Scenario:** Your local community center is struggling to engage teenagers. They observe that fewer teenagers are participating in after-school programs compared to previous years, and the existing programs don't seem to attract new interest.

**Your Task:**

1.  Write a clear and concise problem statement for this scenario.
2.  Identify at least three key stakeholders and their potential concerns.
3.  Define at least three measurable requirements for a program that would address this problem.

By actively practicing problem definition, you build a solid foundation for the entire engineering process. This skill ensures that your engineering efforts are targeted, effective, and truly address the needs of the intended users.
