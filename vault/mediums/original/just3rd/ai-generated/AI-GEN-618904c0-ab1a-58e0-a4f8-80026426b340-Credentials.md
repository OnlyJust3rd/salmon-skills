# Understanding Credentials in Cybersecurity

In cybersecurity, **credentials** are pieces of information that a user, service, or client provides to prove their identity to a system. Think of them as your digital ID card. When you log into a website or an application, you're presenting credentials to show the system "Yes, this is really me."

The primary goal of using credentials is **authentication** – the process of verifying who you claim to be. Without proper authentication, unauthorized individuals could access sensitive data or systems.

## Types of Credentials

Credentials generally fall into a few categories, often used in combination for stronger security:

*   **Something you know:** This is information that only you should know. The most common example is a **password**. Other examples include PINs or security questions (though security questions are often considered weaker due to the possibility of guessing or finding answers).
*   **Something you have:** This is a physical item or a digital token in your possession. Examples include a **security token** (like a YubiKey), a smartphone receiving a one-time code (OTP), or a smart card.
*   **Something you are:** This refers to unique biological characteristics. These are known as **biometrics**, such as fingerprints, facial recognition, or iris scans.

## Practical Example: Logging into Your Email

Let's break down how credentials work when you log into your email account:

1.  **You enter your username:** This is often called an "identifier." It's not a secret, but it tells the system which account you're trying to access.
2.  **You enter your password:** This is the "something you know" credential. The email service's server checks if the password you entered matches the one stored (in a securely hashed form, not plain text!) for that username.
3.  **Authentication successful:** If the password matches, you're authenticated and can access your inbox.

Now, imagine your email service offers **Two-Factor Authentication (2FA)**. This adds another layer:

1.  You enter your username and password (the first factor - "something you know").
2.  The system prompts you for a code from your authenticator app on your phone or a code sent via SMS (the second factor - "something you have" or "something you know" if it's a time-based code you remember).
3.  **Authentication successful with 2FA:** Only if both factors are correct are you granted access. This significantly increases security because even if someone steals your password, they still need access to your second factor.

## Why are Credentials Important?

*   **Preventing Unauthorized Access:** The most crucial role is to stop intruders from getting into systems.
*   **Protecting Sensitive Data:** Your credentials guard personal information, financial details, and confidential business data.
*   **Maintaining System Integrity:** Unauthorized access can lead to data corruption, system damage, or service disruption.
