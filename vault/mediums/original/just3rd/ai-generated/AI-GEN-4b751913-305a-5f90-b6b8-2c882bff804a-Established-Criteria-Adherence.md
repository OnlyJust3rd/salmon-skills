# Evaluating Your Problem-Solving Approach Against Project Criteria

As engineers, we don't just *solve* problems; we select and justify *how* we solve them. This lesson focuses on **Established Criteria Adherence**, a critical step in justifying your chosen engineering problem-solving approach. It’s about ensuring your chosen method aligns perfectly with the specific demands and limitations of your project.

## Why Adherence Matters

Imagine building a bridge. You wouldn't use the same design and materials for a pedestrian walkway as you would for a highway overpass. The project's *criteria* – load capacity, span length, environmental impact, budget, and timeline – dictate the appropriate approach. Similarly, in any engineering project, your chosen problem-solving strategy must be evaluated against a defined set of project parameters.

**Established Criteria Adherence** involves a systematic review process where you:

1.  **Identify** all relevant project criteria.
2.  **Analyze** how your proposed approach meets each criterion.
3.  **Document** this alignment, highlighting strengths and any potential trade-offs.

This evaluation ensures your chosen path is not only technically sound but also practical and achievable within the project's real-world constraints.

## The Criteria Checklist: A Practical Framework

To effectively evaluate adherence, create a checklist based on your project's specific criteria. Common categories include:

*   **Scope:** Does the approach handle the full problem as defined?
*   **Resources:** Is it feasible given available budget, personnel, and equipment?
*   **Timeline:** Can it be implemented within the project schedule?
*   **Performance:** Does it meet required output, efficiency, or quality standards?
*   **Reliability & Safety:** Does it ensure the solution is robust and safe?
*   **Maintainability:** Is the solution easy to manage and update long-term?
*   **Scalability:** Can it adapt to future growth or increased demand?
*   **Ethical/Environmental Impact:** Does it align with ethical guidelines and environmental regulations?

### Worked Example: Developing a New Water Filtration System

Let's say you're part of a team tasked with developing a new, portable water filtration system for disaster relief.

**Project Criteria:**

*   **High Filtration Efficiency:** Must remove 99.9% of bacteria and viruses.
*   **Low Cost:** Target manufacturing cost under $20 per unit.
*   **Ease of Use:** Simple operation for untrained individuals.
*   **Durability:** Withstand rough transport and use in diverse environments.
*   **Portability:** Lightweight and compact.
*   **Scalability:** Design should allow for mass production.

**Proposed Problem-Solving Approach:**

The team proposes an approach centered around a novel ceramic membrane technology combined with a hand-pump mechanism.

## Common Pitfalls to Avoid

*   **Ignoring Less Obvious Criteria:** Don't just focus on the headline requirements. Subtle but critical criteria like maintainability or ethical considerations can derail a project if overlooked.
*   **Vague Assessments:** Instead of saying "meets performance," be specific: "Meets performance by achieving X% efficiency, as demonstrated by Y test."
*   **Failing to Document Trade-offs:** If your approach excels in one area but slightly compromises another, clearly state this and explain why the compromise is acceptable or manageable. For example, a slightly higher manufacturing cost might be justified by significantly improved durability.
*   **Not Revisiting Criteria:** Project needs can evolve. Periodically re-evaluate your approach against the latest project criteria.

By systematically adhering to established criteria, you build a strong, defensible case for your chosen engineering problem-solving approach, paving the way for a successful project outcome.
