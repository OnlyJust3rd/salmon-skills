# Calculating Average Rate of Change

This lesson focuses on calculating the average rate of change, a fundamental concept used in applying the Mean Value Theorem. Understanding this helps us bridge the gap between average and instantaneous changes.

## What is Average Rate of Change?

The average rate of change of a function \(f(x)\) over an interval \([a, b]\) represents the overall change in the function's output divided by the change in its input over that interval. It's essentially the slope of the secant line connecting the two endpoints of the function's graph over that interval.

The formula for average rate of change is:

\[
\text{Average Rate of Change} = \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b - a}
\]

This tells us how much \(f(x)\) changes, on average, for each unit change in \(x\) within the specified interval.

## Practical Example: Car Speed

Imagine you're on a road trip. Your car's odometer shows you've traveled 200 miles in 4 hours. What's your average speed during this time?

Here, our function \(f(t)\) represents the distance traveled at time \(t\).
*   Let \(a = 0\) hours (start of the trip) and \(b = 4\) hours (end of the trip).
*   The distance at \(a\) is \(f(0) = 0\) miles.
*   The distance at \(b\) is \(f(4) = 200\) miles.

Using the formula:

\[
\text{Average Speed} = \frac{f(4) - f(0)}{4 - 0} = \frac{200 \text{ miles} - 0 \text{ miles}}{4 \text{ hours} - 0 \text{ hours}} = \frac{200 \text{ miles}}{4 \text{ hours}} = 50 \text{ miles per hour}
\]

Your average speed over those 4 hours was 50 mph. This doesn't mean you drove at a constant 50 mph; you might have sped up, slowed down, or even stopped, but on average, you covered 50 miles each hour.
