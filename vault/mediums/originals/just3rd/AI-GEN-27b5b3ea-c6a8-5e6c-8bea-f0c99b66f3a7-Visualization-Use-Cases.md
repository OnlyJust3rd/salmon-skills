# Understanding Visualization Use Cases

This lesson focuses on recognizing when to use different types of data visualizations to effectively communicate insights. Understanding these common applications is key to choosing the right tool for your data story.

## What are Visualization Use Cases?

Visualization use cases refer to the typical scenarios or problems where a specific type of chart or graph excels at revealing patterns, trends, or comparisons within data. By understanding these common applications, you can quickly select the most appropriate visualization to convey your message clearly and efficiently.

## Common Visualization Types and Their Use Cases

Let's explore some fundamental visualization types and where they are most commonly applied:

### 1. Bar Charts

*   **What they show:** Comparisons between discrete categories.
*   **Common Use Cases:**
    *   Comparing sales figures across different products.
    *   Showing the number of customers in various regions.
    *   Ranking items (e.g., top performing employees).

**Scenario:** Imagine you're analyzing customer feedback scores for three different service features: "Ease of Use," "Performance," and "Customer Support." A bar chart would be ideal for comparing the average scores of these distinct features side-by-side.

### 2. Line Charts

*   **What they show:** Trends over time or continuous data.
*   **Common Use Cases:**
    *   Tracking stock prices over months.
    *   Monitoring website traffic daily.
    *   Showing temperature changes throughout a year.

**Scenario:** You need to show how your company's monthly revenue has changed over the last fiscal year. A line chart is perfect for illustrating this continuous trend and identifying periods of growth or decline.

### 3. Scatter Plots

*   **What they show:** The relationship or correlation between two numerical variables.
*   **Common Use Cases:**
    *   Investigating the link between advertising spend and sales revenue.
    *   Examining the correlation between study hours and exam scores.
    *   Identifying outliers in data.

**Scenario:** You want to see if there's a relationship between the hours a student spends studying and their final exam grade. A scatter plot, with study hours on one axis and exam grades on the other, can reveal if more study time generally leads to higher grades, or if the relationship is weak or non-existent.

### 4. Pie Charts

*   **What they show:** Proportions of a whole.
*   **Common Use Cases:**
    *   Showing market share distribution among competitors.
    *   Illustrating the breakdown of a budget by category.
    *   Representing the composition of a survey response.

**Scenario:** A marketing team wants to understand the distribution of their advertising budget across different channels (e.g., social media, search ads, print). A pie chart effectively shows how each channel contributes to the total budget.

### 5. Histograms

*   **What they show:** The distribution of a single numerical variable.
*   **Common Use Cases:**
    *   Understanding the frequency of customer ages.
    *   Analyzing the distribution of product prices.
    *   Visualizing the spread of test scores.

**Scenario:** A data analyst is examining the distribution of ages among their user base. A histogram will group users into age brackets (e.g., 18-25, 26-35, etc.) and show how many users fall into each bracket, revealing the typical age range of their users.

## Practice Task

Consider the following scenarios and decide which visualization type would be most appropriate for each:

1.  You want to show how the number of website visitors changed each week for the past quarter.
2.  You are comparing the performance of three different marketing campaigns, measured by the total leads generated by each.
3.  You need to understand if there's a connection between the amount of time customers spend on your app and their purchase frequency.
4.  You are presenting the percentage breakdown of customer service issues (e.g., billing, technical, product inquiry).

## Self-Check Questions

1.  When would you choose a line chart over a bar chart?
2.  What type of visualization is best for showing how parts make up a whole?
3.  If you want to explore the relationship between two different numerical measurements, what visualization would you likely use?
4.  What does a histogram help you understand about a single numerical variable?
