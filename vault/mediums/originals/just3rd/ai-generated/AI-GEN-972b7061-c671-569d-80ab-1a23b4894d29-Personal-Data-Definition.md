# Understanding Personal Data under Thailand's PDPA

This lesson will help you understand what constitutes "personal data" according to Thailand's Personal Data Protection Act (PDPA). This is a crucial first step in interpreting the PDPA's implications for how organizations process information.

## What is Personal Data?

At its core, personal data is any information that relates to an identified or identifiable individual. This means if you can, directly or indirectly, figure out who a person is from the data, it's likely personal data.

The PDPA defines personal data broadly to ensure robust protection.

> **PDPA Definition Snippet (Unofficial Translation):**
> "Personal Data" means any information relating to a natural person, which enables the identification of such person, whether directly or indirectly, but does not include information of a deceased person.

This broad definition is important because it covers a wide range of information that might seem insignificant on its own but can become identifying when combined.

## Identifying Personal Data: Direct vs. Indirect

Let's break down how you can identify an individual:

### Directly Identifiable Data

This is information that clearly points to a specific person without needing any other data.

*   **Examples:**
    *   Full Name (e.g., Somchai Jaidee)
    *   National Identification Number
    *   Passport Number
    *   Email Address (if it includes a name like somchai.jaidee@example.com)
    *   Phone Number

### Indirectly Identifiable Data

This data might not identify someone on its own, but when combined with other information, it can reveal their identity. This is where things get more nuanced.

*   **Examples:**
    *   **Location Data:** If you know someone is consistently at a specific coffee shop every morning, combined with other clues, their identity might be revealed.
    *   **Online Identifiers:** IP addresses, cookie IDs, device IDs. While not a name, these can be used to track and identify individuals online, especially when linked to other profiles or activities.
    *   **Demographic Information:** Age, gender, occupation, marital status, religion. Alone, this isn't enough, but if you have a very specific demographic in a small group, it could lead to identification.
    *   **Physical Characteristics:** Height, weight, eye color, facial features (especially when used for identification like in CCTV footage).
    *   **Behavioral Data:** Online browsing history, purchase history, app usage patterns.
    *   **Biometric Data:** Fingerprints, facial scans, voice recordings. These are highly sensitive and directly link to an individual.

## Special Categories of Personal Data

The PDPA also recognizes "sensitive personal data," which requires even stricter protection due to its potential for causing discrimination or harm. Processing this data typically needs explicit consent or other strong legal bases.

*   **Examples of Sensitive Personal Data:**
    *   Race or ethnicity
    *   Political opinions
    *   Trade union membership
    *   Religious or philosophical beliefs
    *   Sexual orientation or criminal records
    *   Health data
    *   Biometric data (as mentioned above, it falls into both categories)
    *   Disability data

## Practical Examples in a Business Context

Let's consider a few scenarios:

1.  **Online Retailer:**
    *   **Personal Data:** Customer name, shipping address, email, phone number, order history, browsing behavior on the website, payment details.
    *   **Sensitive Personal Data:** If a customer voluntarily provides information about a dietary restriction linked to a health condition (e.g., "Please no nuts, I have a severe allergy").

2.  **Mobile Application:**
    *   **Personal Data:** User's device ID, IP address, app usage patterns, username, email provided for registration, location data (if enabled).
    *   **Sensitive Personal Data:** If the app is for health tracking and collects data on heart rate, sleep patterns, or medication.

3.  **Employee Records:**
    *   **Personal Data:** Employee's full name, address, contact details, bank account for salary, national ID, performance reviews.
    *   **Sensitive Personal Data:** Health information related to sick leave, trade union membership, or information about an employee's criminal record (if legally permissible to collect).

## Key Takeaways

*   **Broad Scope:** The PDPA's definition of personal data is very inclusive. Think "any information that can lead to identifying a person."
*   **Context Matters:** Data that isn't personally identifiable in isolation can become so when combined with other data points.
*   **Indirect Identification is Key:** Be mindful of how seemingly anonymous data can be linked back to individuals, especially with technologies like cookies and device tracking.
*   **Sensitive Data:** Always pay extra attention to the handling of sensitive personal data, as it carries higher risks and requires specific justifications for processing.

Understanding this definition is the foundation for complying with the PDPA and ensuring responsible data handling.
