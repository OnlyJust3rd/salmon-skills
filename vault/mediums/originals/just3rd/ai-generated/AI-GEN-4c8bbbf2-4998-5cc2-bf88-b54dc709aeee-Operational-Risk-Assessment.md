# Operational Risk Assessment

When a business proposes a new project, it's crucial to go beyond just the potential financial gains. We need to understand what could go wrong within the project's internal workings. This is where operational risk assessment comes in. It's about proactively identifying and understanding the threats that can arise from your own processes, people, and systems.

## Why Assess Operational Risks?

Understanding operational risks helps you:

*   **Prevent failures:** By spotting potential issues early, you can put measures in place to stop them from happening.
*   **Minimize impact:** If an issue does occur, having a plan reduces the damage to your project and the business.
*   **Improve efficiency:** Identifying weak points in processes can lead to streamlining and better performance.
*   **Inform decision-making:** A clear understanding of operational risks helps in making realistic projections and allocating resources effectively.

## Identifying Operational Risks

Operational risks stem from the day-to-day execution of a project. They can broadly be categorized into:

### 1. Process Risks

These relate to the design and execution of your project's workflows.

*   **Examples:**
    *   **Inefficient workflows:** A multi-step approval process that causes delays.
    *   **Lack of standardization:** Different teams performing the same task in inconsistent ways, leading to errors.
    *   **Poor quality control:** Insufficient checks at critical stages of production or service delivery.
    *   **Supply chain disruptions:** Reliance on a single supplier who might face issues.

### 2. People Risks

These involve the human element within your project team and organization.

*   **Examples:**
    *   **Skill gaps:** Team members lacking the necessary expertise for a task.
    *   **High staff turnover:** Losing experienced personnel during critical project phases.
    *   **Inadequate training:** Employees not being properly prepared for their roles.
    *   **Internal fraud or negligence:** Deliberate or unintentional errors by staff.
    *   **Communication breakdowns:** Misunderstandings leading to incorrect actions.

### 3. Systems & Technology Risks

These concerns revolve around the technology and infrastructure used to run the project.

*   **Examples:**
    *   **System failures:** Software bugs, hardware malfunctions, or network outages.
    *   **Cybersecurity breaches:** Data theft, unauthorized access, or service disruption due to attacks.
    *   **Outdated technology:** Using systems that are no longer supported or efficient.
    *   **Data integrity issues:** Corruption or loss of critical project data.

### 4. External & Environmental Risks (with an operational impact)

While not purely internal, these external factors can directly disrupt operations.

*   **Examples:**
    *   **Natural disasters:** Flooding, earthquakes, or severe weather affecting physical operations.
    *   **Regulatory changes:** New compliance requirements that necessitate process modifications.
    *   **Utility failures:** Power outages or disruptions to essential services.

## Steps for Operational Risk Assessment

Here's a practical approach to assessing these risks for a proposed project:

### Step 1: Brainstorm and Identify Potential Risks

Gather your project team and key stakeholders. Use the categories above as prompts to brainstorm everything that could potentially go wrong with the internal workings of the project. Don't censor ideas at this stage.

### Step 2: Analyze and Prioritize Risks

For each identified risk, consider:

*   **Likelihood:** How probable is it that this risk will occur? (e.g., Low, Medium, High)
*   **Impact:** If this risk occurs, how severe would the consequences be? (e.g., Minor, Moderate, Major)

You can use a simple matrix to visualize and prioritize:

| Risk                    | Likelihood | Impact | Priority (Likelihood x Impact) |
| :---------------------- | :--------- | :----- | :----------------------------- |
| Lack of trained staff   | Medium     | Major  | High                           |
| Supplier delays         | Low        | Moderate| Medium                         |
| Software bug in core module| Medium     | Moderate| Medium                         |
| Data corruption         | Low        | Major  | Medium                         |

### Step 3: Develop Mitigation Strategies

For the high and medium priority risks, brainstorm ways to reduce their likelihood or impact.

*   **For "Lack of trained staff":**
    *   *Mitigation:* Develop a comprehensive training program before project start, or hire experienced personnel.
*   **For "Supplier delays":**
    *   *Mitigation:* Identify and qualify backup suppliers, maintain buffer stock.
*   **For "Software bug in core module":**
    *   *Mitigation:* Implement rigorous testing protocols, conduct thorough code reviews.
*   **For "Data corruption":**
    *   *Mitigation:* Establish robust data backup and recovery procedures, implement data validation checks.

### Step 4: Assign Ownership and Monitor

Assign a specific person or team responsible for managing each identified risk and its mitigation plan. Regularly review the risk register throughout the project lifecycle to update assessments and identify new risks.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

*   **"It won't happen to us" mentality:** Overconfidence can lead to overlooking critical risks.
*   **Focusing only on the obvious:** Don't neglect less apparent but potentially damaging risks.
*   **Not involving the right people:** Operational risks are often best identified by those who perform the tasks.
*   **Forgetting to monitor:** Risks evolve; what's managed today might need re-evaluation tomorrow.
*   **Creating plans but not implementing them:** Mitigation strategies are useless if they remain on paper.

By systematically assessing operational risks, you build a more resilient project and contribute to a more accurate overall feasibility analysis, ultimately leading to better business decisions.
