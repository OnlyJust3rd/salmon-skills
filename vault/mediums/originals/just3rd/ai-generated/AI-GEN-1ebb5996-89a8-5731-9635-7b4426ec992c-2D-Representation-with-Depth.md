# Creating Depth: Your First Steps in 2D Representation with Depth

Welcome to this lesson on creating a sense of depth in your 2D drawings. We'll focus on how to make flat shapes look like they recede into the distance, a core part of understanding perspective. This is about giving your drawings that "wow" factor by making them feel more real.

## The Illusion of Depth

Think about looking down a long, straight road. The edges of the road seem to get closer together as they stretch away from you, and the trees or buildings along the side appear smaller. This is the illusion of depth, and it's something we can create on a flat piece of paper. We achieve this by carefully drawing lines and shapes so they suggest distance.

### Key Concepts

*   **Horizon Line:** This imaginary line represents your eye level. Objects above the horizon line will appear to be below your eye level, and objects below it will appear above.
*   **Vanishing Point:** This is a point on the horizon line where parallel lines that recede into the distance appear to converge. In one-point perspective, there's typically just one vanishing point.
*   **Receding Lines:** These are the lines in your drawing that are parallel to each other in real life but appear to converge towards the vanishing point on the horizon line.

## Applying Depth to a Simple Shape: The Cube

Let's take a simple geometric shape – a cube – and learn to represent it with depth using one-point perspective. This will involve making a flat square look like a three-dimensional box.

### Step-by-Step Guide

1.  **Draw the Horizon Line:** Lightly sketch a horizontal line across your drawing surface. This is your horizon line.

2.  **Place the Vanishing Point:** Choose a single point on the horizon line. This will be your vanishing point.

3.  **Draw the Front Face of the Cube:** Draw a square on your drawing surface. This square represents the front face of your cube as if you are looking directly at it. Position it either above, below, or intersecting the horizon line.

4.  **Connect Corners to the Vanishing Point:** From each of the **back two** corners of your square (the corners furthest from your viewpoint), draw a light, straight line that extends towards the vanishing point. These are your receding lines.

    *   *Why only two corners?* Because we're showing a face of the cube, and we want to create the illusion of depth extending *away* from that face. If you connected all four corners, you'd create a pyramid.

5.  **Define the Depth (Back Face):** Now, you need to decide how deep your cube will be. Draw a second vertical line and a second horizontal line, parallel to the sides of your original square, that intersect the receding lines you just drew. This creates the back face of your cube. The placement of these lines will determine how deep the cube appears.

6.  **Erase Construction Lines:** Once you are happy with the shape of your cube, carefully erase the parts of the receding lines that extend beyond the back face of the cube.

7.  **Shading (Optional but Recommended):** To further enhance the sense of depth, you can add shading. Imagine a light source. The face directly towards the viewer might be brighter, while the receding side face will be darker, and the top or bottom face (depending on the light source and your cube's position relative to the horizon line) will have a mid-tone.

### Worked Example: Cube Below the Horizon Line

Let's visualize this with a cube positioned below the horizon line.

1.  **Horizon Line:** Draw a horizontal line.
2.  **Vanishing Point:** Mark a dot on the horizon line.
3.  **Front Face:** Draw a square below the horizon line.
4.  **Receding Lines:** From the top-right and bottom-right corners of the square, draw lines towards the vanishing point.
5.  **Back Face:** Draw a vertical line to the left, connecting the two receding lines. Then, draw a horizontal line to the left, connecting the top receding line to the vertical line. This forms the back edge of the cube.
6.  **Clean Up:** Erase the extended receding lines.
7.  **Shading:** The front face can be lightly shaded, the side face darker, and the bottom face the darkest (as it's furthest from the light and perspective).

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

*   **Connecting all four corners:** As mentioned, this creates a pyramid, not a cube.
*   **Lines not meeting the vanishing point:** Ensure your receding lines are drawn accurately towards the single vanishing point.
*   **Uneven depth:** If the lines defining the back face aren't parallel to the front face, your cube will look distorted, not like a true 3D object.
*   **Ignoring the horizon line:** The position of your shape relative to the horizon line is crucial for determining how it's viewed (from above, below, or at eye level).

By practicing this technique with a simple cube, you're building the foundation for creating more complex scenes and objects that have a convincing sense of depth. This is the first step in mastering perspective projection and bringing your 2D representations to life.
