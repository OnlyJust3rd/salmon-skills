# Opportunity Sizing: Estimating Relative Value or Impact

In product strategy, a crucial step after identifying potential features or initiatives is determining which ones offer the most value. This is where **Opportunity Sizing** comes in. It's about estimating the relative value or impact a particular opportunity could bring to your product and your business. This skill helps you prioritize effectively, ensuring you focus your resources on what matters most.

## What is Opportunity Sizing?

Opportunity Sizing is the process of estimating the potential impact, benefit, or value of a product idea, feature, or initiative. It’s not about precise financial forecasting, but rather about understanding the relative scale of different opportunities. This helps you make informed decisions about where to invest your time, effort, and resources.

Think of it like this: If you have a basket of fruits, opportunity sizing is about deciding whether an apple is likely to be more satisfying than a grape, or if a watermelon offers significantly more value than a single berry. You're not measuring exact calories or price per gram, but rather the overall impact each fruit provides.

## Why is Opportunity Sizing Important?

*   **Prioritization:** It provides a basis for ranking opportunities. High-impact opportunities should generally be prioritized over low-impact ones.
*   **Resource Allocation:** Understanding the potential value helps you justify the allocation of development time, marketing budget, and other resources.
*   **Focus:** It keeps your team focused on initiatives that can move the needle for your product and your users.
*   **Strategic Alignment:** It helps ensure that the opportunities you pursue align with your broader product and business goals.

## How to Size Opportunities: Key Considerations and Techniques

Opportunity sizing isn't a single, rigid process. It involves using various methods to get a sense of the potential. The key is to consider different dimensions of value.

### 1. Understand the Dimensions of Value

Opportunities can have value in multiple ways:

*   **Revenue Generation:** Directly increasing sales or subscription revenue.
*   **Cost Reduction:** Saving money through efficiency or automation.
*   **User Acquisition:** Attracting new users to the product.
*   **User Retention/Engagement:** Keeping existing users happy and active.
*   **Market Share Growth:** Expanding your presence in the market.
*   **Strategic Importance:** Opening up new markets, partnerships, or brand perception improvements.

### 2. Estimation Techniques

Here are some practical techniques to estimate opportunity size:

#### a) Top-Down Estimation (Market-Based)

This involves looking at the overall market size and estimating what percentage of that market your opportunity could capture.

*   **Steps:**
    1.  Identify the total addressable market (TAM) for the problem your opportunity solves.
    2.  Estimate the serviceable available market (SAM) – the portion of TAM you can realistically reach.
    3.  Estimate the serviceable obtainable market (SOM) – the portion of SAM you can realistically capture in a given timeframe.
    4.  Translate this market share into potential revenue or user impact.

*   **Example:** You're considering a new feature for your e-commerce app that helps users find sustainable products.
    *   TAM: The entire global e-commerce market for apparel.
    *   SAM: The portion of that market interested in sustainable fashion.
    *   SOM: If you estimate 1% of the SAM could use your feature, and that translates to $X million in potential sales through your platform in the next year.

#### b) Bottom-Up Estimation (User-Based)

This involves estimating the impact on individual users and then scaling up.

*   **Steps:**
    1.  Estimate the number of users who would benefit from the opportunity.
    2.  Estimate the value (e.g., time saved, revenue generated, satisfaction increase) for each of those users.
    3.  Multiply to get the total estimated value.

*   **Example:** A new search filter for your SaaS tool.
    *   Number of users who would use the filter: 10,000 users.
    *   Average time saved per user per month by using the filter: 1 hour.
    *   Value of 1 hour of user time (based on their subscription cost or perceived value): $50.
    *   Total estimated monthly value: 10,000 users * 1 hour/user * $50/hour = $500,000.

#### c) Relative Sizing (T-Shirt Sizing, Story Points)

This is a simpler, often qualitative approach used in agile environments to compare opportunities against each other.

*   **Method:** Assign a size category (e.g., XS, S, M, L, XL or 1, 2, 3, 5, 8) to each opportunity based on its perceived impact or value. This is often done in comparison to other known opportunities.

*   **Example:** You have three ideas:
    1.  **Idea A:** A minor UI tweak. (S)
    2.  **Idea B:** A new reporting dashboard. (L)
    3.  **Idea C:** A partnership integration. (XL)

    You can then visually compare these. The "L" and "XL" opportunities are clearly indicating a larger potential impact than the "S" idea. This doesn't tell you *how much* larger, but it helps in relative ranking.

#### d) Weighted Scoring Models

This is a more structured approach where you define key criteria, assign weights to them, and score each opportunity against these criteria.

*   **Steps:**
    1.  **Define Criteria:** What are the most important dimensions of value for your product? (e.g., Revenue Impact, User Growth, Strategic Alignment, Customer Satisfaction).
    2.  **Assign Weights:** How important is each criterion relative to the others? (Sum of weights should be 1 or 100%).
    3.  **Score Opportunities:** Rate each opportunity against each criterion (e.g., on a scale of 1-5).
    4.  **Calculate Weighted Score:** Multiply the score by the weight for each criterion and sum them up for each opportunity.

*   **Example:**

| Criterion            | Weight | Opportunity 1 Score (1-5) | Opportunity 1 Weighted Score | Opportunity 2 Score (1-5) | Opportunity 2 Weighted Score |
| :------------------- | :----- | :------------------------ | :--------------------------- | :------------------------ | :--------------------------- |
| Revenue Impact       | 0.4    | 4                         | 1.6                          | 3                         | 1.2                          |
| User Growth          | 0.3    | 3                         | 0.9                          | 5                         | 1.5                          |
| Strategic Alignment  | 0.2    | 5                         | 1.0                          | 4                         | 0.8                          |
| Customer Satisfaction| 0.1    | 4                         | 0.4                          | 3                         | 0.3                          |
| **Total Weighted Score** | **1.0** |                           | **3.9**                      |                           | **3.8**                      |

In this example, Opportunity 1 has a slightly higher weighted score, suggesting it might be the better choice based on your defined criteria.

## Common Pitfalls in Opportunity Sizing

*   **Over-reliance on one method:** Mix and match techniques for a more robust estimate.
*   **Ignoring execution effort:** Opportunity size is only half the equation; you also need to consider the effort (cost) to achieve that opportunity.
*   **Bias:** Be aware of personal biases or the "loudest voice in the room" effect.
*   **Lack of data:** While estimations are involved, try to ground them in available data or reasonable assumptions.
*   **"Perfection paralysis":** Don't get bogged down trying to get exact numbers. The goal is relative understanding.

By applying opportunity sizing techniques, you gain clarity on the potential impact of different initiatives, enabling you to make more strategic decisions and drive greater value with your product.
