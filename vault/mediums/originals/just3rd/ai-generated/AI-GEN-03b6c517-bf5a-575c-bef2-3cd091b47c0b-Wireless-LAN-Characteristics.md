# Wireless LAN (WLAN) Characteristics

This lesson focuses on understanding the core properties that define Wireless Local Area Networks (WLANs). This knowledge is crucial for differentiating them from other wireless network types, like Personal Area Networks (PANs), and understanding how they function.

## What is a WLAN?

A Wireless Local Area Network (WLAN) is a wireless networking technology that allows devices to connect to a network without physical cables. It uses radio waves to transmit data, commonly found in homes, offices, schools, and public spaces. The most prevalent standard for WLANs is Wi-Fi.

## Key Characteristics of WLANs

WLANs are defined by several key characteristics that dictate their performance, reach, and application. Understanding these helps us appreciate their design and limitations.

### 1. Range (Coverage Area)

*   **Definition:** The physical distance over which a WLAN signal can reliably reach.
*   **Typical Values:** This can vary significantly based on the Wi-Fi standard, the power of the access point, and environmental factors.
    *   **Indoor:** Generally ranges from **30 meters (about 100 feet)** for older standards to **70-100 meters (about 230-330 feet)** for newer standards within a building. Walls, furniture, and other obstructions reduce this range.
    *   **Outdoor:** Can extend further, but often suffers from interference and is less common for typical WLAN deployments.
*   **Impact:** Determines the size of the area that can be covered by a single access point and influences the number of access points needed for larger spaces.

### 2. Bandwidth (Data Transfer Rate)

*   **Definition:** The maximum rate at which data can be transferred over the WLAN. It's often measured in Megabits per second (Mbps) or Gigabits per second (Gbps).
*   **Evolution:** Bandwidth has dramatically increased with newer Wi-Fi standards (e.g., Wi-Fi 4, 5, 6, 6E, 7).
    *   **Older Standards (e.g., 802.11g):** Around 54 Mbps.
    *   **Current Standards (e.g., 802.11ax/Wi-Fi 6):** Can achieve theoretical speeds of several Gbps, though real-world speeds are lower.
*   **Impact:** Affects how quickly you can download files, stream video, and perform other data-intensive tasks.

### 3. Frequency Bands

*   **Definition:** WLANs operate on specific radio frequency bands. The most common are:
    *   **2.4 GHz:** Offers a longer range and better penetration through walls but has lower bandwidth and is more prone to interference from other devices (microwaves, Bluetooth, cordless phones).
    *   **5 GHz:** Offers higher bandwidth and less interference but has a shorter range and poorer penetration through obstacles.
    *   **6 GHz (Wi-Fi 6E/7):** A newer band offering significantly more bandwidth and less interference, but requires compatible devices.
*   **Impact:** The choice of frequency band affects both the speed and reliability of the connection.

### 4. Security

*   **Definition:** Measures taken to protect the WLAN from unauthorized access and data interception.
*   **Key Protocols:**
    *   **WEP (Wired Equivalent Privacy):** An older, insecure protocol. **Avoid using it.**
    *   **WPA (Wi-Fi Protected Access):** An improvement over WEP, but still has vulnerabilities.
    *   **WPA2:** The current standard for most devices, offering strong encryption (AES).
    *   **WPA3:** The latest standard, providing enhanced security features.
*   **Impact:** Crucial for preventing data breaches and ensuring privacy. Strong security protocols are essential.

### 5. Mobility and Roaming

*   **Definition:** The ability of a device to maintain its network connection as it moves within the coverage area of a WLAN.
*   **How it works:** When a device moves away from one access point, it can seamlessly connect to another access point within the same network (if multiple are deployed). This process is called roaming.
*   **Impact:** Allows users to move around freely within a building or campus while staying connected to the network.

### 6. Interference

*   **Definition:** Signals from other electronic devices operating on similar frequencies that can disrupt or degrade the WLAN signal.
*   **Common Sources:** Microwaves, Bluetooth devices, cordless phones, neighboring Wi-Fi networks, and even certain building materials.
*   **Impact:** Can lead to slower speeds, dropped connections, and reduced reliability.
