# Problem Interview Techniques

When you're building a new product or service, it's crucial to understand if the problem you're trying to solve is real and significant for your potential customers. Problem interviews are a cornerstone of the Lean Startup methodology, allowing you to gather direct feedback and validate your assumptions before investing heavily in development. This lesson focuses on the techniques you'll use to conduct these effective interviews.

## The Goal of Problem Interviews

The primary goal is *not* to sell your idea or get people to say they like it. Instead, you aim to:

*   **Discover Customer Pains:** Uncover genuine frustrations, unmet needs, or inefficiencies that your target customers experience.
*   **Validate Assumptions:** Test your hypotheses about the problem, the customer, and the market.
*   **Understand Context:** Learn how customers currently deal with the problem, what solutions they've tried, and why those solutions are insufficient.
*   **Identify Potential Early Adopters:** Find individuals or businesses who are most affected by the problem and are actively seeking a solution.

## Key Principles for Effective Problem Interviews

Before diving into specific techniques, keep these guiding principles in mind:

*   **Be Curious, Not Persuasive:** Your role is to listen and learn, not to convince. Avoid leading questions.
*   **Focus on the Past, Not the Future:** Ask about past experiences and behaviors. People are often poor at predicting their future actions.
*   **Talk About Their Problems, Not Your Solution:** This is a *problem* interview. You'll get to discussing solutions later.
*   **Seek Specificity:** Vague answers are unhelpful. Dig deeper for concrete examples and details.
*   **Interview as Many People as Possible:** Aim for a diverse range of perspectives within your target customer segment.

## Core Problem Interview Techniques

Here are several techniques to help you conduct impactful problem interviews:

### 1. The "Tell Me About a Time When..." Technique

This is a foundational technique for eliciting genuine stories and understanding context. Instead of asking "Do you have this problem?", you ask about specific instances.

*   **How it works:** Prompt the interviewee to recall a specific situation related to the problem area.
*   **Examples:**
    *   Instead of: "Is managing your inventory difficult?"
    *   Try: "Tell me about a time you had to manage your inventory. What was that experience like?"
    *   Instead of: "Do you struggle with finding reliable contractors?"
    *   Try: "Describe the last time you needed to hire a contractor. What was the process you went through?"

### 2. The "What's the Worst That Could Happen?" Technique

This technique helps uncover the true impact and urgency of a problem. By understanding the negative consequences, you gauge the severity.

*   **How it works:** Ask about the downsides, frustrations, or negative outcomes associated with the current situation or problem.
*   **Examples:**
    *   "What's the worst thing that happens when your software crashes unexpectedly?"
    *   "If you can't resolve this customer support issue quickly, what are the potential repercussions for your business?"
    *   "What are the biggest frustrations you face when trying to [achieve a goal related to your problem]?"

### 3. The "How Do You Solve That Today?" Technique

Understanding existing workarounds and solutions is critical. It reveals the current state of the market and the effort customers are already expending.

*   **How it works:** Inquire about the methods, tools, or processes people currently use to address the problem.
*   **Examples:**
    *   "When you need to track your expenses, what do you do?"
    *   "How are you currently dealing with [the problem you're investigating]?"
    *   "What tools or services are you using to help you with [specific task related to the problem]?"

### 4. The "Why?" Technique (The 5 Whys)

While the name suggests five, the point is to keep asking "why" to peel back layers of understanding and get to the root cause or motivation.

*   **How it works:** When a customer states a problem or a behavior, ask "Why?" repeatedly to probe deeper.
*   **Example:**
    *   Customer: "I hate using spreadsheets for project management."
    *   Interviewer: "Why do you hate using spreadsheets for project management?"
    *   Customer: "Because they get out of sync and it's hard to see who's working on what."
    *   Interviewer: "Why is it hard to see who's working on what in spreadsheets?"
    *   Customer: "Because there are too many different versions and people update them differently."
    *   Interviewer: "Why do people update them differently?"
    *   ...and so on.

### 5. The "Quantify It" Technique

Numbers provide concrete evidence of a problem's scale. If a customer can quantify the impact, it's a strong indicator of a significant pain point.

*   **How it works:** Ask questions that encourage numerical answers about time, money, effort, or frequency.
*   **Examples:**
    *   "How much time do you typically spend on [problematic task] each week?"
    *   "Roughly how much money do you think you lose each year due to [specific inefficiency]?"
    *   "How often does [problematic event] occur?"

## Structuring Your Problem Interview

A good problem interview typically follows a loose structure:

1.  **Introduction & Ground Rules (2-3 mins):**
    *   Introduce yourself and your company (briefly).
    *   Explain the purpose of the interview: to learn about *their* experiences with [problem area].
    *   Emphasize that you're *not* selling anything.
    *   Reassure them that there are no right or wrong answers.
    *   Ask for permission to record (if applicable).

2.  **Open-Ended Exploration (15-20 mins):**
    *   Start with broad, open-ended questions about their role, responsibilities, and the context of the problem area.
    *   Use the "Tell Me About a Time When..." technique to get them talking about specific experiences.
    *   Listen actively for keywords, frustrations, and recurring themes.

3.  **Deep Dive into Pain Points (15-20 mins):**
    *   Once a problem area is identified, use "What's the Worst That Could Happen?", "Why?", and "Quantify It" techniques to understand the severity, root causes, and impact.
    *   Ask about their current solutions using "How Do You Solve That Today?". Understand why those solutions are imperfect.

4.  **Summarize and Next Steps (2-3 mins):**
    *   Briefly summarize what you've heard to ensure you've understood correctly.
    *   Thank them for their time and valuable insights.
    *   Mention that you might follow up with them as you explore potential solutions (this is optional but can set expectations).

## Common Pitfalls to Avoid

*   **Selling Your Solution Too Early:** This derails the learning process and makes the interviewee less honest.
*   **Asking Leading Questions:** Questions that suggest a desired answer (e.g., "Wouldn't it be great if you had X?").
*   **Not Listening:** Interrupting, being distracted, or jumping to conclusions.
*   **Focusing on Hypotheticals:** "What if..." questions are less reliable than past behavior.
*   **Not Interviewing Enough People:** A single conversation isn't enough to draw valid conclusions.

By mastering these problem interview techniques, you equip yourself with the essential skills to uncover real customer needs and build products that truly matter.
