# Common Game Character Archetypes

Welcome to this lesson on identifying common character archetypes used in game design. Understanding these archetypes is a foundational step in character design, helping you recognize patterns and build memorable characters.

## What are Character Archetypes?

Character archetypes are recurring patterns of characters found across stories and media. They represent fundamental human experiences and motivations, making them instantly relatable to audiences. In game design, they serve as useful starting points for creating characters that players can quickly understand and connect with.

## Key Archetypes in Games

Here are some prevalent archetypes you'll encounter:

*   **The Hero:** The protagonist, often thrust into a challenge, who embarks on a journey, faces obstacles, and undergoes growth. They embody courage and determination.
    *   *Example:* Link from *The Legend of Zelda* series.
*   **The Mentor:** A wise, experienced character who guides and advises the Hero. They often possess knowledge or skills crucial for the Hero's success.
    *   *Example:* Master Hand from *Super Smash Bros.* (though a boss, he guides the player through selection).
*   **The Trickster:** A mischievous character who thrives on chaos, often through wit, deception, or clever schemes. They can be allies or antagonists.
    *   *Example:* Loki from *SMITE*.
*   **The Companion:** A loyal friend or ally who supports the Hero. They provide emotional support, practical help, or comic relief.
    *   *Example:* Cortana from the *Halo* series.
*   **The Villain:** The primary antagonist who opposes the Hero, driven by their own motivations, often conflicting with the Hero's goals.
    *   *Example:* Bowser from the *Super Mario* series.
*   **The Shapeshifter:** A character who can alter their form, appearance, or even their allegiances, making them unpredictable.
    *   *Example:* Many enemies in *Dark Souls* that can change attack patterns.

## Practical Example

Imagine you're designing a character for a fantasy RPG. You need a strong warrior to protect the player character. Instead of just drawing a "strong person," you can think of archetypes:

*   Would they be a **Hero** type themselves, perhaps a fallen knight seeking redemption?
*   Or perhaps a **Mentor**, an older, grizzled warrior who trains the player?
*   Could they be a **Companion**, a loyal bodyguard with a hidden past?

By starting with an archetype, you gain an immediate understanding of the character's core motivations, typical behaviors, and how they might interact with others.

## Practice Task

Watch the intro cinematic or play the first 10 minutes of a game you're familiar with. Identify at least three characters and try to assign them one of the archetypes listed above.
