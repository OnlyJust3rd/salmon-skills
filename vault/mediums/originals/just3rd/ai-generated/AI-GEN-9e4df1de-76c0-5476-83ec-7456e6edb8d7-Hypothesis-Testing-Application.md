# Hypothesis Testing with the Build-Measure-Learn Cycle

This lesson focuses on the practical application of the Build-Measure-Learn (BML) cycle for testing your core business assumptions. By actively testing, you move beyond guesswork and gain validated learning about your customers and your product.

## What are Hypotheses in Lean Startup?

At its heart, the Lean Startup methodology relies on testing assumptions. These assumptions are your "hypotheses" – educated guesses about how your business will work. They typically fall into a few key categories:

*   **Value Hypotheses:** Do customers actually find value in your product or service? Will they use it?
*   **Growth Hypotheses:** How will new customers discover, acquire, and retain your product or service?
*   **Problem/Solution Fit Hypotheses:** Does your product genuinely solve a real problem for a defined group of customers?

### Example Hypothesis

Let's say you're building a mobile app to help local musicians find gigs.

*   **Hypothesis:** "Local musicians will pay $5 per month for a premium feature that automatically suggests suitable gigs based on their genre and availability."

This hypothesis is specific, measurable, and testable.

## Applying the Build-Measure-Learn Cycle for Hypothesis Testing

The BML cycle provides the framework for systematically testing these hypotheses.

### 1. Build: Create a Minimum Viable Product (MVP)

The goal here is not to build a perfect, fully-featured product. Instead, you build the smallest possible version that allows you to test your core hypothesis.

*   **For our musician app example:**
    *   **MVP Idea 1 (Landing Page Test):** Create a simple landing page describing the premium feature and its benefits. Include a "Sign Up for Early Access" button.
    *   **MVP Idea 2 (Concierge MVP):** Manually match musicians with gigs using spreadsheets and phone calls. This simulates the app's core function without building any code.
    *   **MVP Idea 3 (Wizard of Oz MVP):** Build a basic interface where musicians can input their preferences, but behind the scenes, a human is actually performing the gig-finding.

The choice of MVP depends on what you want to test and how quickly you can build it. A landing page is quick to build and tests demand. A concierge MVP tests the core value proposition more directly.

### 2. Measure: Gather Data and Feedback

Once your MVP is out in the world, it's time to measure its performance and collect data. This data will tell you whether your hypothesis is holding up.

*   **For our musician app example (using the Landing Page MVP):**
    *   **Quantitative Data:** Track the number of visitors to the landing page, the click-through rate on the "Sign Up" button, and the number of sign-ups.
    *   **Qualitative Data:** Conduct short interviews with people who visit the page but don't sign up. Ask them why. If they sign up, follow up with a brief survey about what they expect from the premium feature.

It's crucial to define what "success" or "failure" looks like for your hypothesis *before* you start measuring. For instance, if you aimed for a 5% conversion rate on your landing page, and you only get 1%, that's a strong signal.

### 3. Learn: Analyze and Iterate

This is where you turn your data into actionable insights. You analyze the measurements to understand whether your initial hypothesis was correct, partially correct, or completely wrong.

*   **For our musician app example:**
    *   **Scenario A: Hypothesis Validated:** You get a 7% conversion rate on the landing page, and the follow-up surveys indicate strong interest in the proposed features.
        *   **Learning:** Musicians are likely willing to pay for this service.
        *   **Next Step (Build):** Proceed to build a more functional MVP of the app, focusing on the features users expressed interest in.
    *   **Scenario B: Hypothesis Partially Validated:** You get a 2% conversion rate, but interviews reveal musicians are interested in *finding* gigs but not necessarily willing to pay a monthly subscription. They might prefer a per-gig commission or a one-time fee.
        *   **Learning:** The problem (finding gigs) is real, but the proposed solution (paid subscription) might be flawed.
        *   **Next Step (Pivot/Iterate):** Re-evaluate the pricing model or the exact features offered. Perhaps build a prototype that tests a commission-based model.
    *   **Scenario C: Hypothesis Invalidated:** You get a 0.5% conversion rate, and interviews suggest musicians aren't actively looking for a dedicated app or that existing methods are sufficient.
        *   **Learning:** The core assumption that musicians need and will pay for this specific solution is likely incorrect.
        *   **Next Step (Pivot/Persevere):** Consider pivoting to a different problem for musicians, or a different customer segment altogether. Or, if you believe strongly in the problem, persevere by testing completely different solutions.

## Key Principles for Hypothesis Testing with BML

*   **Be Specific:** Vague hypotheses lead to vague results.
*   **Focus on Behavior, Not Opinions:** What people *do* is more important than what they *say*.
*   **Test the Riskiest Assumptions First:** Identify your biggest "bet" and test it early.
*   **Embrace Failure as Learning:** An invalidated hypothesis is not a failure; it's a learning opportunity that saves you time and resources.
*   **Iterate Quickly:** The power of BML is its speed. Aim to complete cycles rapidly.

By consistently applying the Build-Measure-Learn cycle to test your hypotheses, you gain confidence in your business direction and reduce the risk of building something that nobody wants.
