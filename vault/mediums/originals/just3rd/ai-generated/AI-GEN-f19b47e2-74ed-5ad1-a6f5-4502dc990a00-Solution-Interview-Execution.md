# Executing Effective Solution Interviews

This lesson focuses on the practical execution of solution interviews, a critical part of gathering user feedback on your Minimum Viable Product (MVP). By applying specific techniques, you can uncover valuable insights to refine your solution.

## Why Solution Interviews?

Solution interviews are your direct line to understanding how potential users perceive and interact with your proposed solution. Unlike general user interviews that explore problems, solution interviews focus on the specific features and value proposition of your MVP. This helps you validate your assumptions and identify areas for improvement before significant development effort is invested.

## Planning Your Solution Interview

Before you even speak to a user, thorough preparation is key.

### Define Your Goals
What specific questions do you need answered? Are you trying to understand:
*   The clarity of your value proposition?
*   The ease of use of a particular feature?
*   The perceived effectiveness of your solution in addressing a problem?
*   Potential barriers to adoption?

### Develop Your Interview Script (Guide)
While you don't want to sound robotic, a script provides structure and ensures you cover all essential areas.

*   **Introduction:** Briefly introduce yourself, the purpose of the interview (to gather feedback on a new solution), and assure them their feedback is valuable and confidential. Obtain consent to record if applicable.
*   **Context Setting:** Briefly remind them of the problem you're trying to solve (without leading them too much towards your specific solution).
*   **Solution Introduction:** Clearly and concisely explain your MVP. This could involve a short demo, a prototype walkthrough, or a detailed description of its core functionality.
*   **Probing Questions:** This is the core of your interview. Focus on open-ended questions that encourage detailed responses.
*   **Wrap-up:** Thank the participant for their time and provide any necessary next steps or follow-up information.

### Example Probing Questions:

*   "What are your initial thoughts when you see/hear about this solution?"
*   "How does this compare to how you currently solve [the problem]?"
*   "Which parts of this solution are most appealing to you? Why?"
*   "Are there any parts of this solution that are confusing or unclear?"
*   "Imagine you were using this for the first time. What would you expect to happen when you click [this button/perform this action]?"
*   "What concerns, if any, do you have about using this solution?"
*   "What features are missing that would make this even more valuable to you?"
*   "On a scale of 1 to 5, how likely would you be to use this solution? (Follow up with 'why'.)"

### Select Your Participants
Ensure your participants align with your target user profile. Recruit them strategically to get diverse perspectives.

## Conducting the Interview: On the Fly

Once you're in the interview, your role shifts to active listening and skillful facilitation.

### Create a Comfortable Environment
Make the participant feel at ease. A relaxed participant is more likely to share honest feedback.

### Active Listening is Key
*   **Pay attention:** Focus on what the participant is saying, both verbally and non-verbally.
*   **Nod and use verbal cues:** Phrases like "Uh-huh," "I see," and "Tell me more" encourage them to continue.
*   **Avoid interrupting:** Let them finish their thoughts before jumping in.
*   **Clarify and summarize:** Periodically rephrase what you've heard to ensure you understand correctly. "So, if I understand correctly, you're saying that..."

### Probing Techniques for Deeper Insights

*   **The "Why" Technique:** When a participant states an opinion or a preference, ask "Why?" multiple times (but not aggressively) to get to the root cause.
    *   *Participant:* "I don't like this button."
    *   *You:* "Why not?"
    *   *Participant:* "It's too small."
    *   *You:* "Why does the size of the button matter to you?"
*   **The "Tell me more" Technique:** This is a gentle way to encourage elaboration.
    *   *Participant:* "This feature is confusing."
    *   *You:* "Could you tell me more about what you found confusing?"
*   **Observational Questions:** If you are observing them interact with a prototype, ask questions about their actions.
    *   "What were you expecting to happen when you clicked there?"
    *   "What were you trying to achieve when you did that?"

### Handling Difficult Situations

*   **Silence:** Don't be afraid of silence. It often gives the participant time to think and volunteer more information.
*   **Leading Questions:** Be mindful of your phrasing. Avoid questions that suggest a desired answer (e.g., "You think this feature is great, right?"). Instead, use neutral phrasing like "What are your thoughts on this feature?"
*   **Uncertainty:** If a participant is unsure, acknowledge it and explore their uncertainty. "It's okay not to be sure. What makes you hesitant?"

## Post-Interview Analysis

After the interview, your work isn't done.

### Review Your Notes and Recordings
Thoroughly go through your notes and recordings as soon as possible while the conversation is fresh.

### Identify Key Themes and Insights
Look for recurring patterns, strong opinions, common pain points, and valuable suggestions. Categorize your findings.

### Document Your Findings
Clearly document what you learned, supported by direct quotes from participants where possible. This documentation will be crucial for informing your MVP's development and for planning usability testing.
