# Guidance for Proof of Concept (PoC) Development using Build-Measure-Learn

This lesson focuses on how to apply the Build-Measure-Learn (BML) cycle specifically when developing a Proof of Concept (PoC). A PoC is a small-scale experiment designed to test the feasibility of a specific idea or technology.

## Understanding the Goal of a PoC in BML

Within the Lean Startup framework, a PoC isn't about building a polished product. Its primary purpose is to **learn quickly and cheaply** whether a core assumption or hypothesis is valid. You're not aiming for a Minimum Viable Product (MVP) that a customer can use; you're aiming for evidence that your core idea is worth pursuing further.

## The Build Phase: Crafting Your PoC

The "Build" phase for a PoC is about creating the *absolute minimum* to test your riskiest assumption.

### Key Considerations for PoC Builds:

*   **Focus on the Core Hypothesis:** What is the single most critical thing you need to prove or disprove? Your PoC should be built around testing *that*.
*   **Simplicity is Paramount:** Avoid complex features, extensive UI/UX work, or robust backend systems. Think "sketches" or "prototypes" rather than functional software.
*   **Speed Over Perfection:** The faster you can build and test, the faster you learn. Don't get bogged down in details that don't directly impact your core hypothesis.
*   **External vs. Internal PoCs:**
    *   **External:** Designed to get feedback from potential users on the core value proposition. This might involve mockups, landing pages with signup forms, or even a "concierge MVP" where you manually deliver the service.
    *   **Internal:** Designed to test technical feasibility or a specific process. This might be a small script, a data model simulation, or a simplified integration between two systems.

### Example PoC Build Scenarios:

*   **Hypothesis:** Users will find value in an app that automatically categorizes their expenses.
    *   **PoC Build:** Create a simple landing page with a clear description of the app. Include a signup form to gauge interest. You might even manually categorize a few sample expenses for early signups to demonstrate the concept.
*   **Hypothesis:** A new algorithm can significantly improve the accuracy of fraud detection.
    *   **PoC Build:** Develop a standalone script that runs the new algorithm on a limited, anonymized dataset and compares its results to existing methods. Focus on the algorithm's core logic and output, not a full-fledged fraud detection system.

## The Measure Phase: Gathering Meaningful Data

The "Measure" phase for a PoC is about collecting data that directly speaks to your core hypothesis. The metrics should be unambiguous and directly related to what you're trying to learn.

### What to Measure in a PoC:

*   **Direct Validation Metrics:** These measure whether your hypothesis is proving true.
    *   *For the expense app PoC:* Number of signups, conversion rate from landing page visit to signup, qualitative feedback from early testers.
    *   *For the fraud detection PoC:* Percentage improvement in detection accuracy compared to the baseline, number of false positives/negatives.
*   **Effort/Cost Metrics:** Understanding how much it cost (time, money) to build and test your PoC is crucial for future decisions.
*   **Qualitative Feedback:** Even for internal PoCs, understanding *why* something worked or didn't work is invaluable.

### Avoiding Vanity Metrics:

For a PoC, avoid metrics that look good but don't inform your core learning. For example, website "hits" are less valuable than actual signups or expressed interest for the expense app.

## The Learn Phase: Iterating and Deciding

The "Learn" phase is where you analyze the data from the Measure phase and make informed decisions about the next steps.

### Key Learning Questions for a PoC:

*   **Did our core hypothesis hold true?** (Yes/No/Partially)
*   **What specific insights did we gain?** (e.g., users loved feature X, but feature Y was confusing.)
*   **What are the riskiest assumptions remaining?**
*   **Based on this learning, should we:**
    *   **Pivot:** Change direction based on new information.
    *   **Persevere:** Continue with the current direction, perhaps with minor adjustments.
    *   **Stop:** Abandon the idea if the PoC clearly demonstrates it's not viable.

### Applying Learning to PoCs:

*   **If the PoC confirms your hypothesis:** You've reduced risk and can now consider building an MVP. Your next build phase will be more robust.
*   **If the PoC disproves your hypothesis:** You've saved significant resources by learning early. Analyze *why* it failed and use those learnings to inform your next idea or pivot. Perhaps the problem statement was wrong, or the solution approach was flawed.
*   **If the PoC is inconclusive:** You may need to design another, slightly more complex PoC to gather more specific data.

## Conclusion

Applying the Build-Measure-Learn cycle to PoC development is about efficient, targeted learning. By focusing on your riskiest assumptions and building the simplest possible solution to test them, you can quickly gather evidence to either validate your idea and move forward, or recognize its limitations and pivot before investing heavily. This iterative approach is fundamental to de-risking innovation.
