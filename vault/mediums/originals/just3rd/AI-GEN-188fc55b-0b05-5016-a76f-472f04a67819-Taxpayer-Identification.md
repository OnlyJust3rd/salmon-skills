# Taxpayer Identification

In the world of income tax, understanding *who* needs to pay is the very first step. This lesson focuses on remembering the key individuals and entities that are obligated to file and pay income tax.

## What is a Taxpayer?

A taxpayer is any individual or entity that is legally required to pay taxes to a government. For income tax, this means anyone or any organization that earns income above a certain threshold.

## Key Individuals and Entities That Are Taxpayers

Let's break down the main categories of taxpayers.

### 1. Individuals

This is the most common category. If you earn income, you are likely an individual taxpayer.

*   **Residents:** Individuals who live in a country for a significant period, usually defined by specific rules (e.g., time spent in the country, having a permanent home there), are generally taxed on their worldwide income.
*   **Non-Residents:** Individuals who do not meet the residency criteria but earn income within the country are typically taxed only on that income earned within the country's borders.
*   **Citizens:** While citizenship is important, tax obligations are often more closely tied to residency. However, some countries tax their citizens on worldwide income regardless of where they live.

**Examples:**

*   An employed person receiving a salary.
*   A freelancer earning income from various clients.
*   A retiree receiving pension or social security benefits.
*   An individual earning rental income from a property.

### 2. Businesses and Other Entities

Income tax isn't just for individuals. Many organizations also have tax obligations.

*   **Sole Proprietorships:** Owned and run by one individual. The business income is taxed as the owner's personal income.
*   **Partnerships:** Owned and operated by two or more individuals (or entities). The partnership itself generally doesn't pay income tax, but the partners report their share of the partnership's profits on their individual tax returns.
*   **Corporations (C-corps):** These are separate legal entities. Corporations pay income tax on their profits. Then, if profits are distributed to shareholders as dividends, those dividends are taxed again at the shareholder level.
*   **S-Corporations (in the US):** These are corporations that elect to pass corporate income, losses, deductions, and credits through to their shareholders for federal tax purposes. This avoids the "double taxation" of C-corporations.
*   **Limited Liability Companies (LLCs):** The tax treatment of an LLC depends on how it is structured and elects to be taxed. It can be treated as a sole proprietorship, partnership, or corporation for tax purposes.
*   **Estates and Trusts:** When a person passes away, their estate may be required to pay income tax on income earned between the date of death and the distribution of assets. Trusts, which hold assets for beneficiaries, are also often required to pay income tax on income they earn.
*   **Non-profit Organizations:** While many non-profit organizations are exempt from income tax, they may still have reporting requirements or pay tax on unrelated business income.

**Examples:**

*   A local bakery owned by one person (Sole Proprietorship).
*   A law firm with multiple partners (Partnership).
*   A large technology company (Corporation).
*   A small business that has chosen pass-through taxation (S-Corporation or LLC).
*   The financial assets managed by a deceased person's representative (Estate).
*   A trust set up to provide for children's education (Trust).

## Key Takeaway

Remembering these categories is fundamental. The specific rules and tax rates vary significantly by country and by the type of entity, but the core principle remains: if you earn income, you are likely a taxpayer in some capacity. Understanding your status as an individual or entity taxpayer is the first step in fulfilling your tax obligations correctly.
