# Understanding Why Business Actions Are Unethical

Some business practices harm individuals, stakeholders, or society, making them unethical. Understanding the *why* behind these judgments is crucial for responsible business conduct. This lesson explores the rationale behind deeming certain business behaviors as unethical.

## Core Concepts: The Harm Principle

At its heart, unethical business behavior often stems from causing undue harm. This harm can manifest in various ways:

*   **Financial Harm:** Cheating customers, investors, or employees out of money.
*   **Reputational Harm:** Damaging trust and credibility through deceit.
*   **Physical/Well-being Harm:** Endangering the health or safety of individuals.
*   **Societal Harm:** Undermining fair competition, legal frameworks, or public good.

## Examples and Their Rationale

Let's look at common unethical behaviors and why they are considered wrong:

*   **Bribery:** Offering or accepting something of value to influence a decision.
    *   **Rationale:** It corrupts fairness and merit. Decisions should be based on objective criteria, not personal gain. This harms competitors and erodes public trust in institutions.

*   **Plagiarism:** Presenting someone else's work or ideas as your own.
    *   **Rationale:** It violates intellectual property rights and is a form of theft. It disrespects the original creator's effort and can mislead others about the originality of the work.

*   **Child Labor:** Employing individuals below the legal working age, often in dangerous or exploitative conditions.
    *   **Rationale:** It deprives children of their right to education, health, and a normal childhood. It is a severe exploitation that causes long-term physical and psychological damage.

*   **Accounting Fraud:** Manipulating financial records to mislead stakeholders about a company's performance.
    *   **Rationale:** This deceives investors, creditors, and employees. It undermines market integrity, can lead to significant financial losses for those who rely on accurate financial information, and often involves breaking the law.

*   **False Advertising:** Making deceptive or unsubstantiated claims about products or services.
    *   **Rationale:** It tricks consumers into making purchasing decisions based on false information. This harms consumers by causing them to spend money on inadequate products and harms honest competitors who advertise truthfully.

## Practical Scenario

Imagine a small tech startup developing a new app. They are struggling to get investors. To make their financial projections look more promising, they inflate the projected user numbers and revenue significantly in their pitch deck, without any concrete basis.

**Why is this unethical?**
This is a form of deception. The startup is causing potential investors to make financial decisions based on misleading information. If the investors lose money, it's due to the startup's unethical presentation. It also damages the credibility of the startup and the startup ecosystem if discovered.

## Practice Task

Think about a product you've seen advertised. Did the advertisement accurately represent the product? Or did it seem to exaggerate its benefits? Write down one example and explain, based on the principles discussed, why the advertisement might be considered misleading or unethical.
