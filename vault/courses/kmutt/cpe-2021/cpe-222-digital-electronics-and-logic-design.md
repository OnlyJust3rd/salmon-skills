# CPE 222 - Digital Electronics and Logic Design

Logic families, simple A/D and D/A conversions, Boolean algebra, combinational logic circuits, modular design of combinational circuits, memory elements, sequential circuits and state machines, driver and register chips, tri-state, modeling and simulation, building real circuits with CAD tools, functional blocks, programmable devices. Breadboard building of combinational and sequential circuits, use of multimeter and oscilloscope, oscillator and power regulators, noise and power issues. Creation of a state machine from programmable devices.

## Course Competencies

- Explain and apply Boolean algebra, De Morgan's theorems, and basic logic gates to analyze and simplify logic expressions.
- Utilize Karnaugh Maps (K-maps) to optimize and minimize multi-level combinational logic circuits.
- Understand the fundamental principles of interfacing digital systems with the real world, specifically through simple Analog-to-Digital (A/D) and Digital-to-Analog (D/A) conversions.
- Analyze and design modular combinational logic circuits (including adders, subtractors, multiplexers, and decoders), and verify their logical behavior and correctness using Proteus 8.
- Analyze and design clocked sequential logic circuits, including flip-flops, counters, memory devices (RAM/ROM), and state machines.
- Translate real-life problems into truth functions and conceptualize digital circuit models to address those specific problems.
- Construct and troubleshoot logic circuits on breadboards using discrete components and standard ICs.
- Operate standard laboratory equipment-such as multimeters, oscilloscopes, oscillators, and power regulators-to measure circuit parameters and test functionality safely.
- Identify and mitigate physical hardware challenges, such as noise interference and power regulation issues, when verifying physical designs.
- Model digital circuits using Verilog Hardware Description Language (HDL) and create a testbench to verify the correctness of the hardware design in software simulation before applying it to a real FPGA.
- Apply different levels of abstraction in Verilog-specifically gate-level, dataflow, and behavioral modeling-to design combinational and sequential modules.
- Program programmable logic devices (such as FPGAs) to deploy and test digital designs, bridging the gap between successful simulation and physical hardware implementation.
